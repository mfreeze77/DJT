# Chapter 7 Open Questions and Records Plan
## January 20, 2025–July 18, 2026

This file converts unresolved analytical questions into document requests, data tasks, and falsifiable tests. The goal is not to ask agencies to answer “Was Trump influenced by Russia?” Agencies should be asked for identifiable existing records concerning a decision, meeting, directive, transaction, or assessment.

---

## 1. Highest-priority unanswered questions

### Q1. What exactly was paused in March 2025?

The public record confirms a military-aid and intelligence-sharing pause and later restoration, but not its complete scope.

**Records needed**

- Signed policy or implementation directives.
- Date-and-time activation and termination records.
- Weapons, contracts, shipments, and authorities affected.
- Intelligence products, systems, agencies, and dissemination channels affected.
- Legal memoranda.
- Operational and civilian-harm risk assessments.
- Battlefield-impact assessments.
- Dissent, concurrence, and escalation memoranda.
- Communications with NATO and Ukraine concerning the pause.

**Primary custodians likely to possess agency records**

- Department of Defense, including Office of the Secretary of Defense and relevant military components.
- Department of State.
- Office of the Director of National Intelligence and relevant intelligence agencies.
- Defense Security Cooperation Agency.
- Department inspectors general.

**Important limit**

Presidential and immediate White House records are governed by the Presidential Records Act rather than ordinary agency FOIA access during the administration. NARA states that presidential records are transferred when a president leaves office and generally become eligible for FOIA access five years later, subject to restrictions. Request agency copies now and preserve the White House-record question for later PRA access.

### Q2. What was discussed or offered at the February Riyadh meeting?

**Records needed**

- Memoranda of conversation.
- Delegation lists.
- Briefing books and talking points.
- Draft agendas and proposed outcomes.
- Cables to and from U.S. embassies in Riyadh, Moscow, Kyiv, and relevant European capitals.
- Readouts sent to Ukraine and NATO allies.
- Records of Russian proposals concerning territory, NATO, sanctions, assets, elections, energy, or reconstruction.
- Records showing whether any U.S. official represented that Ukraine would accept a particular term.

**Primary custodian**

Department of State. Its official FOIA guidance recommends identifying record type, timeframe, subject, country, originating/receiving offices, and the event that created the record.

**Suggested request frame**

> Records created or received from February 10 through February 28, 2025 concerning Secretary Rubio’s Riyadh meetings with Sergei Lavrov and Yuri Ushakov, including memoranda of conversation, briefing memoranda, talking points, draft or final readouts, proposed negotiating terms, interagency communications, and communications with Ukraine or NATO allies.

Do not request “all Russia records.” Use the named meeting, participants, offices, and date range.

---

### Q3. Why did the United States change its U.N. position on February 24?

**Records needed**

- State Department voting instructions.
- Draft resolution language and revision history.
- Legal-adviser analysis.
- Communications with the U.S. Mission to the United Nations.
- Communications with Russia, Ukraine, France, the United Kingdom, and other Security Council members.
- Interagency memoranda explaining the change in terminology.
- Assessments of expected Russian and allied reactions.
- Any linkage to ceasefire, sanctions, prisoner, or economic negotiations.

**Primary custodians**

- Department of State.
- U.S. Mission to the United Nations records maintained by State.
- Relevant agency copies of interagency communications.

**Falsifiable test**

If records show the language was a U.S.-origin consensus strategy developed independently and paired with firm private sovereignty assurances, the foreign-influence inference weakens. If records show Russian-proposed wording, undisclosed reciprocal terms, or ignored intelligence warnings, concern strengthens.

### Q4. What was the value and legal mechanism of the Black Sea economic relief?

**Records needed**

- Treasury and State option papers.
- Office of Foreign Assets Control analyses and licenses.
- Agriculture and Commerce Department assessments where applicable.
- Banking, payment, port, insurance, and shipping implementation records.
- Estimates of commercial value.
- Conditions precedent and Russian reciprocal obligations.
- Compliance and violation reports.
- Communications with financial institutions, insurers, shippers, and foreign governments.
- Records explaining whether relief concerned sanctioned activity or over-compliance affecting permitted food trade.

**Primary custodians**

- Treasury, especially OFAC and international-affairs offices.
- State Department.
- Departments of Agriculture and Commerce where records exist.
- Coast Guard or Transportation components for maritime implementation.

**Falsifiable test**

Narrow technical measures tied to verified Russian compliance would support the inducement/food-security explanation. Broad relief delivered without reciprocal performance would strengthen the concession critique.

### Q5. What commitments were made in Trump-Putin calls and the Alaska summit?

**Records needed**

- Memoranda of telephone conversation.
- Interpreter notes.
- Participant lists.
- Briefing and after-action memoranda.
- Intelligence preparation and damage/risk assessments.
- Commitments communicated to State, DOD, Treasury, NATO, Ukraine, or Congress.
- Draft summit statements and rejected language.
- Records of any discussion of sanctions, Russian assets, territory, elections, NATO, energy, or private business interests.

**Access path**

Agency-created or agency-controlled copies should be requested from the relevant agencies. Presidential originals will require later access under the Presidential Records Act, subject to classification and statutory restrictions.

**Research discipline**

Until the records exist publicly, do not invent quotations, letters, promises, or private concessions.

### Q6. Were Ukraine’s substitute security guarantees credible?

**Records needed**

- Draft and final security-guarantee texts.
- Enforcement triggers.
- Force, air-defense, intelligence, financing, and weapons commitments.
- Duration and termination clauses.
- U.S., European, NATO, and Ukrainian legal analyses.
- Ukrainian acceptance, reservations, and redlines.
- Russian objections or claimed rights to veto implementation.

**Falsifiable test**

Detailed, funded, enforceable guarantees would weaken the claim that excluding NATO membership left Ukraine exposed. Vague political assurances with no enforcement would strengthen it.

### Q7. What was actually delivered through PURL and other aid mechanisms?

**Data fields**

- Package number and approval date.
- Funding state.
- U.S. contracting authority.
- System, quantity, and value.
- Contract award.
- Production status.
- Delivery date.
- Training and sustainment.
- Ukrainian receipt.
- End-use monitoring.
- Operational use where releasable.

**Sources**

- NATO.
- DOD and DSCA.
- Allied defense ministries.
- U.S. contracting databases.
- Congressional notifications.
- Ukrainian defense records.

**Reason**

“Pledged,” “procured,” “delivered,” and “used” are different claims.

---

### Q8. Did the India tariff materially reduce Russian oil revenue?

**Data needed**

- Indian customs imports by origin and grade.
- Tanker ownership, insurance, flag, and ship-to-ship transfer data.
- Refinery intake and export data.
- Imports through intermediaries.
- Russian realized prices and discounts.
- U.S. tariff collections and importer costs.
- Substitution toward other Russian-oil buyers.

**Falsifiable test**

A sustained decline in Indian direct and indirect Russian-oil intake would support the administration’s claimed result. Routing changes without reduced Russian revenue would weaken it.

---

### Q9. Does URIF create durable support or problematic leverage?

**Records needed**

- Complete fund agreement and annexes.
- Board rules and minutes.
- Preferred-share valuation protocols.
- Conflict-of-interest and ethics rules.
- Beneficial owners of project applicants and recipients.
- Procurement and offtake arrangements.
- Project-level diligence.
- Political-risk insurance agreements.
- Returns, fees, defaults, and performance.
- Rules excluding suppliers of Russia’s war machine.

**Tests**

- Does the fund mobilize new capital?
- Are investments transparent and competitive?
- Do projects strengthen Ukraine or primarily transfer resource value?
- Do Trump-associated or politically connected entities participate?
- Are Russian or sanctions-linked beneficial owners excluded in practice?

No allegation of self-dealing should be made without project-level evidence.

---

### Q10. What did U.S. intelligence assess about Russian influence efforts in 2025–2026?

**Records needed**

- Finished intelligence suitable for declassification.
- Intelligence-community assessments.
- Foreign-malign-influence warnings.
- Counterintelligence threat assessments.
- Notifications to campaigns, officials, Congress, or allies.
- Assessments of Russian media amplification, disinformation, cyber activity, economic influence, and cultivation of intermediaries.
- Any assessment connecting an influence operation to a particular policy decision.

**Likely obstacles**

Classification, sources-and-methods protections, ongoing investigations, and presidential-communications issues will limit release. Request finished, disseminated, segregable records rather than operational files or source identities.

## 2. Suggested federal request modules

These should be filed separately by component. Do not submit one omnibus request.

### Module A — State Department: Riyadh and U.N. diplomacy

```text
Pursuant to FOIA, I request records created or received from
February 10 through March 5, 2025 concerning:

1. Secretary Marco Rubio's meetings in Riyadh with Sergei Lavrov
   and Yuri Ushakov;
2. U.S. drafting, negotiation, voting instructions, or legal analysis
   concerning the February 24, 2025 United Nations General Assembly
   and Security Council resolutions on Ukraine; and
3. communications with the governments of Russia or Ukraine, NATO,
   or Security Council members concerning those meetings or resolutions.

Requested record types include memoranda of conversation, briefing
memoranda, talking points, cables, draft and final readouts, resolution
drafts and revision histories, legal analyses, and interagency
communications.

Please search the Office of the Secretary, relevant regional and
international-organization bureaus, the U.S. Mission to the United
Nations, and U.S. embassies in Riyadh, Moscow, and Kyiv.
```

This module may still be too large for efficient processing. Filing Riyadh and U.N. requests separately is preferable.

### Module B — DOD: March support pause

```text
I request records created from February 28 through March 12, 2025
concerning the suspension, review, limitation, or restoration of
military assistance or intelligence support to Ukraine.

Requested records include signed directives, implementation messages,
legal memoranda, lists of affected assistance and shipments, risk or
battlefield-impact assessments, start and termination times,
communications with Ukraine or NATO, and final decision memoranda.
```

### Module C — Treasury: Black Sea commercial measures

```text
I request records created from March 1 through June 30, 2025
concerning the United States commitment announced March 25, 2025 to
help restore Russia's access to world markets for agricultural and
fertilizer exports, lower maritime-insurance costs, and enhance access
to ports and payment systems.

Requested records include option papers, legal analyses, OFAC analyses
or licenses, economic valuations, implementation plans, compliance
conditions, reciprocal-performance requirements, and communications
with financial institutions, insurers, shipping companies, or foreign
governments.
```

### Module D — DOD/DSCA: PURL implementation

```text
I request records sufficient to identify each Prioritised Ukraine
Requirements List package involving U.S. defense articles from
July 1, 2025 through July 18, 2026, including package value, funding
country, systems and quantities, contracting authority, contract or
case number, approval date, production status, delivery status, and
end-use monitoring.
```

Ask for a structured export if such a database exists; agencies need not create a new record.

## 3. Non-FOIA and later-access routes

### Presidential Records Act

NARA explains that official presidential and vice-presidential records are U.S. property, transfer to NARA at the end of the administration, and generally become eligible for FOIA access five years later, subject to restrictions that can extend longer. Preserve a list now of:

- call memoranda;
- presidential briefing materials;
- Oval Office notes;
- summit records;
- White House messaging records;
- presidential schedules;
- envoy reporting; and
- decision memoranda.

### Congress

Congress is not subject to FOIA. Useful routes include:

- oversight requests and hearings;
- committee reports;
- member letters and agency responses;
- appropriations and authorization reporting requirements;
- inspector-general referrals;
- Government Accountability Office work; and
- voluntary committee releases.

### Courts

Federal courts are not subject to FOIA. Use PACER, court clerks, unsealing motions, and National Archives holdings for litigation concerning sanctions, tariffs, contracts, records disputes, or implementation.

### NATO, Ukraine, and foreign governments

Use official disclosure regimes, parliamentary questions, national freedom-of-information laws, audit institutions, procurement databases, and treaty-organization releases. Each jurisdiction has different exemptions and access rules.

## 4. Open analytical questions

1. Did the U.N. shift originate with Trump, Rubio, Witkoff, another U.S. official, or a Russian proposal?
2. Did any private U.S. assurance differ materially from public support for Ukrainian sovereignty?
3. Were sanctions, frozen assets, election issues, private business, or domestic investigations discussed in leader calls?
4. Were intelligence warnings overridden or incorporated?
5. What reciprocal Russian act was expected for each concession?
6. Did Russia comply with any energy, maritime, humanitarian, or ceasefire commitment?
7. Did personal envoys bypass or complement professional diplomatic channels?
8. What information reached Trump before each reversal?
9. Did any financial interest of Trump, his family, donors, partners, or associates overlap with a decision?
10. Did Russian intelligence or media tailor messages to Trump’s known preferences?
11. Which administration actions imposed the largest measurable cost on Russia?
12. Which actions imposed the largest measurable cost on Ukraine?
13. Did allied spending increases strengthen deterrence enough to offset rhetorical uncertainty?
14. Were the proposed security guarantees acceptable to Ukraine and enforceable against future aggression?
15. What portions of the policy survived because of institutional constraints rather than presidential preference?

## 5. Evidence thresholds for future revisions

### To strengthen a foreign-influence finding

Seek evidence of all three:

1. **Actor:** an identifiable Russian state, intelligence, oligarchic, criminal, or proxy actor;
2. **Mechanism:** money, threat, kompromat, information operation, secret contact, inducement, or intermediary; and
3. **Decision linkage:** contemporaneous evidence that the mechanism affected a particular U.S. decision.

### To strengthen a control finding

The threshold is higher: authenticated instructions, acknowledged agency relationship, coercive leverage shown to determine conduct, quid pro quo records, or convergent intelligence and witness evidence. Policy similarity alone is insufficient.

### To weaken the hypothesis

Evidence that decisions were:

- developed independently through a documented interagency process;
- supported by conventional military, economic, or diplomatic analysis;
- resisted or penalized by Russia;
- paired with substantial private pressure on Moscow;
- corrected after adverse results; or
- unrelated to any foreign contact or financial interest.

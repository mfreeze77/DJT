# Chapter 2 Supporting Research

## From StB Access to Soviet Cultivation, Organized-Crime Exposure, and Financial Vulnerability (1977–1990)

## Research purpose

This supporting document expands the existing Chapter 2 research from the foundation established in the merged Chapter 1 package. It does not treat the 1987 Moscow visit as an isolated event. It reconstructs the longer sequence by which:

1. Czechoslovak intelligence obtained recurring access to Trump through Ivana and her family;
2. Trump independently developed political and Soviet-negotiation ambitions;
3. Soviet diplomats approached him with status, flattery, and a Moscow hotel opportunity;
4. the Moscow trip was followed by rapid national political activity;
5. Trump’s New York and Atlantic City businesses operated alongside organized-crime, narcotics, opaque-property, and casino-compliance risks; and
6. Eastern Bloc access persisted into direct Czechoslovak institutional contact.

The central analytical model is **converging channels**, not one all-purpose conspiracy.

# 2. Expanded chronology

## 1977–1979: family access becomes intelligence access

- **April 1977:** Donald Trump marries Ivana Zelníčková.
- The marriage draws StB attention through the Zlín/Gottwaldov regional office.
- Miloš Zelníček becomes a recurring “conspiratorial” source under pressure.
- **1979:** during Ivana’s return visit, StB reporting calls for operational attention.
- Archive reporting says a memorandum was copied to the First Directorate section responsible for “illegals” abroad.

### Significance

The Eastern Bloc gained access to Trump before he became internationally prominent. The information channel did not require Trump’s awareness or cooperation.

## 1980–1985: Trump’s target profile becomes public

- Trump opens the Grand Hyatt and completes Trump Tower.
- His public profile expands from local real-estate developer to national celebrity businessman.
- Presidential possibilities are discussed in interviews well before 1987.
- **1985:** Mike Wallace confronts Trump with his claim that he could negotiate arms control with the Soviets more effectively than professional negotiators.
- Trump responds that, if it were him, he could do it.

Source: [CBS News, 1985 Mike Wallace interview](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/mike-wallace-grills-donald-trump-on-his-political-comments-in-1985-mike-wallace-is-here-movie/).

### Significance

The Soviet service did not need to invent a desire for international political influence. Trump was publicly displaying it.

## 1985–1986: Russia fixation before the invitation

- Bernard Lown, co-recipient of the 1985 Nobel Peace Prize for anti-nuclear work, meets Gorbachev.
- Lown later recalls Trump seeking an assessment of Gorbachev and saying he wanted Reagan to appoint him a special ambassador capable of quickly ending the Cold War.
- The recollection is retrospective, but it is consistent with the contemporaneous Mike Wallace exchange.

Source: [History News Network reproduction of Lown account](https://www.historynewsnetwork.org/article/trump-angled-for-soviet-posting-in-the-1980s).

### Classification

**Retrospective first-person testimony corroborated in theme by contemporaneous evidence.**

## 1986: Dubinin’s approach

- Yuri Dubinin becomes Soviet ambassador to the United States.
- Trump meets Dubinin in New York.
- Dubinin flatters Trump by praising Trump Tower.
- Later accounts by Dubinin’s family describe the approach as calculated and effective.
- Discussion shifts to a potential Soviet hotel project.

### Significance

The approach matched both Soviet doctrine and Trump’s visible preferences: prestige, monumental construction, direct leader-to-leader negotiation, and international recognition.

## January–July 1987: invitation and controlled access

- Trump receives a letter reporting that Soviet tourism authorities are interested in a joint hotel project.
- In July, Donald and Ivana Trump visit Moscow and Leningrad through Intourist.
- The couple receives VIP treatment and reviews potential development sites.
- The visit occurs in a Soviet state-security environment where important foreign visitors were routinely monitored and assessed.

### Missing primary records

- visa and biographical questionnaire;
- Intourist invitation and internal approval;
- complete itinerary;
- guide and driver identities;
- hotel and room assignments;
- meeting participant list;
- site-selection memoranda;
- visitor-assessment reports;
- diplomatic cables;
- KGB/First Chief Directorate operational file.

## September–October 1987: political activation

- **September 2:** full-page Trump advertisements appear in *The Washington Post*, *The New York Times*, and *The Boston Globe*.
- Cost reported contemporaneously: **$94,801**.
- The advertisements attack the cost of defending allies and urge hard financial demands on Japan, Western Europe, Saudi Arabia, and other states.
- Public-relations advisers connected to Reagan’s 1984 campaign help develop the presentation; Trump says he wrote the message.
- **October 22:** Trump gives a political address in Portsmouth, New Hampshire, before a crowd that had been primed by a Draft Trump campaign.
- Trump denies running while delivering a candidate-style speech centered on weakness, disrespect, trade, allies, and leadership.

Sources:

- [Washington Post, September 2, 1987](https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/politics/1987/09/02/between-the-lines-of-a-millionaires-ad/9c6db9c3-f7d6-4aa4-9ec4-a312feb2639e/)
- [Los Angeles Times, October 23, 1987](https://www.latimes.com/archives/la-xpm-1987-10-23-mn-10766-story.html)

### Research implication

The timing supports post-trip activation. Domestic authorship and preexisting beliefs prevent the stronger claim that the advertisements were necessarily Soviet-authored.

## 1980–1983: concrete and labor exposure

- Trump Tower uses S&A Concrete.
- Later federal litigation establishes that the Manhattan concrete “Club” allocated jobs and used control of concrete supply and labor.
- A federal appellate decision identifies S&A entities as instruments of the conspiracy.
- Roy Cohn represents Trump while also representing Anthony Salerno.
- Local 282 and John Cody control critical construction-delivery leverage.

Primary source: [State of New York v. Julius Nasso Concrete Corp., 202 F.3d 82](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/F3/202/82/592729/).

## 1984–1992: Bogatin proceeds enter Trump Tower

- **1984:** David Bogatin buys five Trump Tower units for nearly $6 million.
- **1987:** Bogatin pleads guilty in the gasoline-tax fraud matter.
- He flees the United States and is later extradited.
- State authorities seize the Trump Tower properties.
- Public reporting on the forfeiture describes them as assets used to conceal or launder proceeds.

### Documentary distinction

The purchase and criminal case are established. The complete forfeiture order and deed-level flow should replace generalized secondary descriptions once obtained.

## 1985–1990: Weichselbaum relationship persists after indictment

- **October 1985:** Joseph Weichselbaum is federally indicted.
- He informs a Trump aide of the charges.
- Two months later he rents a Trump-owned apartment.
- His aviation company continues servicing casino transportation.
- He pleads guilty to cocaine-distribution and tax charges.
- Trump writes a sentencing character letter.
- Weichselbaum receives a three-year sentence and serves approximately eighteen months.
- His girlfriend acquires adjoining Trump Tower units during his imprisonment; he later resides there.
- **1990:** New Jersey gaming regulators question Trump about the relationship and the letter.

Primary and documentary sources:

- [United States v. Weichselbaum, 853 F.2d 923](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/F2/853/923/121325/)
- [Damin Aviation Corp. v. Sikorsky Aircraft, 705 F. Supp. 170](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/district-courts/FSupp/705/170/1978442/)
- [The Smoking Gun document archive](https://www.thesmokinggun.com/documents/celebrity/the-donald-and-the-dealer-173892)
- [DGE interview and letter archive](https://www.thesmokinggun.com/documents/celebrity/trump-dge-report-weichselbaum-563821)

## 1988–1989: StB strategic collection intensifies

- Ivana predicts George H. W. Bush’s election during a visit.
- StB analysts treat the accuracy as evidence of access to high-level U.S. circles.
- **January 23, 1989:** a secret collection plan seeks information on U.S. foreign policy, politics, economics, and intelligence agencies.
- The family channel has now become an explicit strategic collection program.

## October 1989–November 1990: Slušovice contact

- A Slušovice delegation meets Trump in Trump Tower.
- StB collaborator “Al Jarda” reports on the interaction.
- Trump is invited to visit the cooperative.
- In November 1990, while attending Miloš Zelníček’s funeral, Trump visits Slušovice operations and meets František Čuba.

Sources:

- [Guardian 2016 StB file report](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2016/dec/15/czechoslovakia-spied-on-donald-trump-ivana-files)
- [Guardian 2018 operational reconstruction](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2018/oct/29/czechoslovakia-spied-on-trump-to-exploit-ties-to-highest-echelons-of-us-power)

## April 1990–December 1991: Taj Mahal reporting failures

- Trump Taj Mahal begins operation in April 1990.
- FinCEN later finds failures to file required casino currency-transaction reports between April 1990 and December 1991.
- The 1998 settlement is $477,000 and follows an IRS compliance examination.

Primary source: [FinCEN 1998 enforcement announcement](https://www.fincen.gov/news/news-releases/fincen-announces-penalty-against-trump-taj-mahal-associates).

# 4. Target-profile reconstruction

## Publicly visible traits before Moscow

### Ambition

Trump entertained national political attention while denying immediate candidacy.

### Negotiator self-conception

He believed personal force and business bargaining could solve nuclear and geopolitical problems that had resisted professional diplomats.

### Prestige sensitivity

Trump Tower, public rankings, famous contacts, and international recognition were central to his brand.

### Commercial appetite

A hotel near the Kremlin was both a lucrative proposition and an extraordinary branding opportunity.

### Transactional worldview

Trump interpreted alliances, security, diplomacy, and government through payment, leverage, and deal-making.

### Tolerance for opaque intermediaries

His domestic development environment already involved fixers, controlled industries, and counterparties whose backgrounds presented legal or reputational risk.

## Why this profile matters

Cultivation succeeds most readily when it reinforces existing drives. The strongest model is not brainwashing or sudden ideological conversion. It is repeated reward of predictable preferences.

# 5. Political-message comparison

## Trump’s 1987 public themes

- U.S. allies exploit American protection.
- Japan’s economic success rests partly on U.S. defense spending.
- wealthy states should pay the United States directly.
- American politicians are weak and allow humiliation.
- a forceful negotiator could reverse decline.

## Soviet strategic benefit

A Soviet government seeking division in the Western system would benefit from:

- reduced U.S. commitment to alliances;
- conflict between Washington and Tokyo or Western Europe;
- public distrust of professional diplomats and security institutions;
- a transactional rather than values-based alliance system; and
- the rise of an American public figure advocating those ideas.

## Domestic explanations

The same themes can arise independently from:

- economic nationalism;
- trade conflict with Japan;
- resentment of Gulf burden-sharing;
- Reagan-era conservative debates;
- Trump’s business branding;
- book promotion; and
- a political trial balloon.

## Current assessment

The alignment is relevant because it followed a targeted Soviet approach and controlled visit. Alignment alone is not proof of tasking. The project should seek communications, drafts, calendars, adviser notes, and Soviet reporting that could establish whether contact altered the message.

# 6. Domestic organized-crime exposure

## Roy Cohn

### Documented role

- Trump’s lawyer and strategic mentor.
- Counsel to Anthony Salerno and other organized-crime figures.
- Public defender of aggressive, retaliatory tactics.
- Ultimately disbarred for conduct involving dishonesty, fraud, deceit, and misrepresentation.

### Analytical role

Cohn reduced the social and transactional distance between Trump and individuals controlling construction, unions, politics, and media.

## S&A Concrete

### Primary finding

The Second Circuit records that a cartel known as the “Club” allocated concrete jobs and that S&A entities were instruments of the conspiracy.

### Trump connection

Trump Tower used S&A Concrete.

### Proper conclusion

Trump’s project was embedded in a judicially established cartel environment. The available primary record does not show that Trump was a member of the criminal enterprise.

## John Cody and Local 282

### Existing evidence

Documentary reporting describes Cody’s control over delivery labor and an apartment benefit involving a woman associated with him.

### Records needed

- FBI file and classification;
- subpoena and grand-jury derivative records where releasable;
- trial exhibits;
- apartment deed and closing file;
- Trump Organization ledger entries;
- Local 282 records;
- construction delivery logs;
- government closing memorandum.

# 7. Foreign and criminal capital

## David Bogatin

### Established facts

- five Trump Tower units purchased for nearly $6 million;
- major gasoline-tax fraud case;
- guilty plea;
- flight and extradition;
- seizure of units.

### Investigative value

The case provides a concrete example of a Trump-branded property being used to store and conceal proceeds generated by Soviet-émigré organized crime.

### Limits

- Trump’s knowledge at closing is not established.
- A direct business link between Donald Trump and Semion Mogilevich is not established by the condominium closing alone.
- The complete chain of funds is not yet reconstructed.

# 9. Casino AML findings

## 1990–1991 violations

FinCEN’s 1998 announcement establishes failure to timely file required CTRC forms during the Taj Mahal’s opening period.

## What it proves

- large-currency reporting controls failed;
- the failures were sufficient for a civil penalty;
- IRS examination identified the violations.

## What it does not prove

- every transaction involved criminal money;
- Russian criminals were responsible for the cited reports;
- Trump personally ordered nonfiling;
- the later 2003–2015 AML violations existed in identical form in 1990.

# 10. Strongest new findings

## Finding 1: political ambition predates Moscow

The 1985 Wallace exchange and 1986 Lown account show that Trump had already cast himself as a potential Soviet arms-control negotiator. This supports a targeted-amplification model.

## Finding 2: Moscow followed a decade of allied-service access

Chapter 1’s 1979 First Directorate distribution finding changes the context of the Dubinin approach. Soviet access arrived after years of StB observation.

## Finding 3: the 1987 sequence is more consequential than a simple business trip

The hotel invitation, controlled visit, national advertisements, and New Hampshire speech form a coherent political-activation sequence.

## Finding 4: domestic vulnerability was already normalized

Trump’s reliance on Cohn, S&A, controlled labor, and risky counterparties suggests an established comfort with intermediaries carrying coercive or reputational power.

## Finding 5: Trump Tower was usable for criminal asset concealment almost immediately

Bogatin’s five-unit purchase is a property-level example, not merely a later statistical pattern.

## Finding 6: Weichselbaum was not cut off after indictment

The apartment, aviation, casino, character-letter, and later residential connections demonstrate continuing accommodation after Trump’s organization had a pathway to know of the charges.

## Finding 7: currency-reporting failures began at the Taj Mahal’s opening

FinCEN’s official date range begins in April 1990, anchoring casino-compliance vulnerability at the transition between Chapters 2 and 3.

## Finding 8: Eastern Bloc access became institutional

Slušovice shows that the channel extended beyond Ivana’s family into direct commercial contact monitored by StB collaborators.

# 11. Evidence boundaries

The expanded research should not be misread as establishing facts not yet documented.

- No public KGB file establishes formal recruitment.
- No public record establishes that Trump knowingly accepted intelligence tasking.
- No public record proves the KGB wrote the September 1987 advertisement.
- No public record identifies specific kompromat from the 1987 trip.
- No public record establishes Soviet financing of Trump’s 1980s expansion.
- No court has found Trump guilty of participating in the concrete cartel.
- No current record proves Trump knew Bogatin’s purchase money was illicit.
- No current record proves the Weichselbaum apartments were hush money or payment.
- No current record proves all described actors belonged to one coordinated network.

These limits define records targets; they do not erase the convergence of documented access and vulnerability.

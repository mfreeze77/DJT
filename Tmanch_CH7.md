# Chapter 7: Trump’s Second Administration, Russia, Ukraine, and NATO  
## January 20, 2025–July 18, 2026

> **Research status:** Rebuilt from primary and near-primary records in July 2026.  
> **Supersedes:** The prior Chapter 7 draft, which mixed documented history with prospective scenarios and contained factual errors.  
> **Related files:** [claim ledger](Tmanch_CH7_claim_ledger.csv) · [primary-source inventory](Tmanch_CH7_primary_sources.md) · [counterevidence](Tmanch_CH7_counterevidence.md) · [open questions](Tmanch_CH7_open_questions.md) · [supporting notes](Tmanch_CH7_support.md)

## Method and evidentiary limits

This chapter distinguishes five different propositions that are often collapsed into one:

1. **A policy objectively benefits Russia.**
2. **A policy reflects an ideological or strategic preference shared with Moscow.**
3. **A policy creates a vulnerability that Russia could exploit.**
4. **Russian actors influenced the decision through contact, leverage, or information operations.**
5. **Donald Trump knowingly acted under Russian direction or control.**

The public record can establish some instances of the first three. It does **not**, as of July 18, 2026, establish the fifth. The fourth remains an appropriate counterintelligence question in several areas, but it cannot be treated as proven without records connecting Russian activity to a specific decision.

Official White House releases are primary evidence of what the administration announced, did, or represented. They are not neutral verification of the administration’s motives, private negotiations, or claimed diplomatic successes. NATO, United Nations, Treasury, Defense Department, State Department, Ukrainian-government, congressional, and court records are used to test those representations.

## Executive findings

### Finding 1 — The original Chapter 7 required complete replacement

The earlier draft openly said it blended history with “plausible extrapolation,” then described projections as events. It also misidentified Richard Grenell as National Security Advisor and dated Trump’s address to a joint session of Congress as February 28, 2025.

The contemporary record shows that **Mike Waltz** was National Security Advisor on February 11, 2025, and that Trump’s joint address occurred on **March 4, 2025**. Waltz was later nominated and confirmed as U.S. ambassador to the United Nations; Marco Rubio subsequently served simultaneously as Secretary of State and National Security Advisor. See the [White House statement signed by Waltz](https://www.whitehouse.gov/briefings-statements/2025/02/statement-from-the-national-security-advisor/), the [March 4 address](https://www.whitehouse.gov/videos/president-trump-addresses-joint-session-of-congress-march-4-2025/), and the [Senate confirmation record for Waltz](https://www.senate.gov/legislative/LIS/roll_call_votes/vote1191/vote_119_1_00530.htm).

### Finding 2 — Early 2025 produced a marked diplomatic and rhetorical shift toward Moscow

The administration rapidly reopened direct high-level contact with Russia, held bilateral talks with Russian officials in Riyadh, changed the framing used by the United States at the United Nations, and sought a negotiated settlement on an accelerated timetable.

On February 24, the United States opposed a General Assembly resolution centered on a “comprehensive, just and lasting peace” and Ukraine’s territorial integrity, while later securing Security Council adoption of a shorter resolution that did not identify Russia as the aggressor. Russia publicly welcomed the change in the U.S. position. The votes did not recognize Russia’s territorial claims, but they materially reduced the diplomatic isolation and condemnatory framing Moscow had faced. See the [General Assembly record](https://press.un.org/en/2025/ga12675.doc.htm), the [Security Council record](https://press.un.org/en/2025/sc16005.doc.htm), and the [post-veto debate](https://press.un.org/en/2025/ga12677.doc.htm).

### Finding 3 — The administration used U.S. support as leverage against Ukraine

After the contentious February 28 Oval Office meeting, the United States paused military assistance and intelligence sharing. Ukraine then accepted the U.S. proposal for an interim thirty-day ceasefire, contingent on Russian reciprocity, and the United States immediately restored intelligence sharing and security assistance on March 11.

The sequence is established. The complete internal legal and policy rationale, decision memoranda, scope of the intelligence cutoff, and assessment of battlefield consequences have not been publicly released. See the [official video of the February 28 meeting](https://www.whitehouse.gov/videos/president-trump-and-ukrainian-president-zelenskyy-in-oval-office-feb-28-2025/) and the [U.S.-Ukraine joint statement published by Ukraine’s presidency](https://www.president.gov.ua/en/news/spilna-zayava-za-pidsumkami-zustrichi-delegacij-ukrayini-ta-96553).

### Finding 4 — Some negotiating choices offered Russia concrete diplomatic or economic benefits

The March 25 U.S.-Russia Black Sea statement said the United States would help restore Russia’s access to world agricultural and fertilizer markets, reduce maritime-insurance costs, and improve access to ports and payment systems. That was a concrete U.S. commitment to facilitate economic relief as part of the negotiating process.

The parallel U.S.-Ukraine statement contained maritime, energy, humanitarian, and peace-process commitments but no comparable market-access concession. This does not prove corrupt intent; it does show that early diplomacy contemplated benefits sought by Moscow before a comprehensive settlement was reached. Compare the [U.S.-Russia statement](https://www.whitehouse.gov/briefings-statements/2025/03/outcomes-of-the-united-states-and-russia-expert-groups-on-the-black-sea/) with the [U.S.-Ukraine statement](https://www.whitehouse.gov/briefings-statements/2025/03/outcomes-of-the-united-states-and-ukraine-expert-groups-on-the-black-sea/).

### Finding 5 — The later record contradicts a one-direction “all policy favored Russia” account

During the same period, the administration and NATO took actions inconsistent with a simple theory of Russian policy control:

- The United States and Ukraine created a reconstruction investment fund that Treasury described as a commitment to a “free, sovereign, and prosperous Ukraine.”
- Trump directed additional defensive weapons to Ukraine in July 2025.
- The United States and NATO launched the PURL mechanism to supply U.S. weapons funded by European and Canadian allies.
- Trump imposed an additional 25 percent tariff on Indian imports because of India’s purchase of Russian oil, later removing it after India committed to stop those imports.
- NATO reaffirmed Article 5, identified Russia as a long-term threat, raised the defense-investment benchmark to 5 percent of GDP, and continued large-scale support for Ukraine.
- In July 2026, NATO pledged €70 billion in military equipment, assistance, and training for Ukraine in 2026 and at least an equivalent level in 2027.

Those actions do not erase the early concessions or pressure on Kyiv. They demonstrate that the evidence supports a **mixed, personalized, and internally inconsistent policy**, not a clean story of uninterrupted accommodation.

### Finding 6 — No final peace settlement existed by July 18, 2026

The Alaska Trump-Putin summit, subsequent meetings with Zelenskyy and European leaders, Geneva negotiations, and later talks produced diplomatic frameworks and continued engagement but no publicly documented final settlement. NATO continued to describe Russia’s war and attacks as ongoing in 2026 and continued supplying Ukraine.

# 1. Corrected baseline: personnel, dates, and scope

## 1.1 National-security personnel

Mike Waltz served as National Security Advisor during the administration’s opening Russia and Ukraine diplomacy. A February 11 White House statement concerning the release of Marc Fogel is expressly attributed to “National Security Advisor Mike Waltz.”

Waltz later left that role and was nominated to represent the United States at the United Nations. The Senate confirmed him on September 19, 2025. By 2026, official congressional proceedings identified Marco Rubio as both Secretary of State and the President’s National Security Advisor.

This correction matters because the earlier draft attributed early policy to the wrong official and relied on prospective reporting rather than appointments as they actually occurred.

## 1.2 Correct date of the joint address

Trump addressed a joint session of Congress on March 4, 2025—not February 28. February 28 was the date of the Oval Office confrontation with President Volodymyr Zelenskyy. Conflating those events distorted the chronology linking the confrontation, aid pause, and March 11 negotiations.

## 1.3 Scope

This chapter assesses:

- U.S.-Russia diplomacy;
- U.S. policy toward Ukraine;
- sanctions and economic pressure;
- military assistance;
- NATO commitments;
- official descriptions of the war;
- institutional checks and allied burden sharing; and
- what these facts do and do not establish concerning foreign leverage.

It does not attempt to reconstruct unrelated domestic policy or every global event of the second administration.

# 2. January–February 2025: diplomatic opening and changed framing

## 2.1 Marc Fogel exchange and early engagement

On February 11, the White House announced that Special Envoy Steve Witkoff was leaving Russian airspace with detained American Marc Fogel. Waltz described the exchange as Russian “good faith” and a sign of movement toward ending the war.

The release was a real diplomatic achievement. It also showed that Witkoff had direct access to Russian decision-makers at the opening of the administration.

## 2.2 Riyadh talks without Ukraine

The State Department’s historical travel record shows that Rubio visited Riyadh from February 17 through 19 and met Russian Foreign Minister Sergei Lavrov and presidential aide Yuri Ushakov alongside Waltz and Witkoff. Ukraine was not a participant in that bilateral U.S.-Russia meeting.

Direct diplomacy with an adversary is not itself evidence of improper influence. The concern is procedural and strategic: discussing a war settlement with the aggressor before integrating the invaded state and European allies can shift negotiating leverage toward Moscow. The records needed to determine what commitments were contemplated remain largely nonpublic.

## 2.3 The February 24 United Nations votes

At the General Assembly, two resolutions were adopted. One reaffirmed Ukraine’s sovereignty and territorial integrity and called for a comprehensive, just, and lasting peace. The United States voted against that Ukraine- and Europe-backed resolution. The Assembly also amended a U.S. draft before adopting it.

At the Security Council later that day, the United States secured adoption of Resolution 2774 by ten votes to zero, with five European members abstaining. Russia vetoed proposed amendments referencing Ukraine’s sovereignty and territorial integrity and a comprehensive, just, and lasting peace. Russia’s representative welcomed the change in Washington’s position.

**What is established:** the United States changed both its vote and diplomatic vocabulary in a way Moscow welcomed.

**What is not established:** that the vote was dictated by Russia, that the United States recognized annexed territory, or that a secret bargain caused the change.

## 2.4 February 28 Oval Office confrontation

Trump and Vice President JD Vance publicly confronted Zelenskyy in the Oval Office. The White House later promoted statements praising Trump and Vance for “standing up for America.” The meeting did not produce the planned minerals agreement that day.

The event matters less as political theater than as the immediate context for the suspension of assistance and intelligence sharing. It visibly personalized the alliance dispute around gratitude, bargaining posture, and presidential deference rather than limiting the disagreement to written policy.

# 3. March 2025: pressure, partial reset, and asymmetrical bargaining

## 3.1 Aid and intelligence pause

The administration paused portions of military assistance and intelligence sharing after the February 28 meeting. Public records do not yet provide:

- the signed directive;
- a complete list of affected intelligence products;
- the agencies and systems covered;
- the operational risk assessment;
- the exact start and end times;
- the legal authorities used; or
- the administration’s assessment of battlefield effects.

Those gaps are central to evaluating proportionality and motive.

## 3.2 Jeddah and restoration of support

On March 11 in Jeddah, Ukraine accepted a U.S. proposal for an immediate interim thirty-day ceasefire, extendable by agreement and conditioned on Russia’s concurrent acceptance. The joint statement said the United States would immediately lift the intelligence-sharing pause and resume security assistance.

The agreement repaired the immediate rupture and placed a ceasefire proposal before Russia. It also demonstrates that U.S. security support had been made conditional during negotiations with the invaded state.

## 3.3 March 19 Trump-Zelenskyy call

A White House statement said Trump briefed Zelenskyy on his conversation with Putin; the leaders discussed an initial halt to attacks on energy and other civilian infrastructure, battlefield information sharing, air-defense needs, humanitarian exchanges, and the possibility of U.S. involvement with Ukrainian energy facilities.

The statement is evidence of the administration’s account. It does not independently establish Russian compliance with any partial ceasefire.

## 3.4 March 23–25 Black Sea talks

The United States conducted separate technical talks with Russia and Ukraine in Riyadh.

Shared elements included:

- safe navigation;
- preventing military use of commercial vessels;
- developing measures against attacks on energy facilities; and
- continued peace negotiations.

The U.S.-Ukraine statement also stressed prisoners, civilian detainees, and forcibly transferred children. The U.S.-Russia statement added U.S. help restoring Russian agricultural and fertilizer access, lowering insurance costs, and improving ports and payment access.

That economic-relief commitment is one of the strongest documented facts supporting concern that the administration was prepared to deliver benefits sought by Moscow before Russia accepted a complete ceasefire or withdrew from occupied territory. Whether that was a tactical inducement, a necessary food-market measure, or an excessive concession is an analytical judgment; the fact of the commitment is not.

# 4. April–July 2025: institutional ties to Ukraine and competing policy currents

## 4.1 U.S.-Ukraine Reconstruction Investment Fund

On April 30, Treasury announced an agreement creating the U.S.-Ukraine Reconstruction Investment Fund. Treasury said the partnership centered on a free, sovereign, and prosperous Ukraine and barred those who financed or supplied Russia’s war machine from benefiting.

The White House described equal U.S.-Ukrainian board representation and a structure receiving a share of revenue from new natural-resource projects. The arrangement gives the United States a financial interest in Ukraine’s long-term survival and recovery. It may also create commercial conflicts or distribution disputes; those require separate examination of the agreement and project-level due diligence.

## 4.2 NATO’s Hague Summit

On June 25, NATO leaders:

- reaffirmed an “ironclad” Article 5 commitment;
- identified Russia as a long-term threat to Euro-Atlantic security;
- committed to spending 5 percent of GDP on defense and security-related requirements by 2035; and
- affirmed continued support for Ukraine.

This is important counterevidence to the prior draft’s prediction that NATO’s future was simply in doubt. Trump’s burden-sharing pressure contributed to a substantially higher spending pledge, while the alliance’s collective-defense commitment remained intact.

## 4.3 July aid review and reversal

On July 2, the Defense Department confirmed that some assistance to Ukraine and other states was paused during a capability review intended to protect U.S. stockpiles. On July 7, the Pentagon announced that, at Trump’s direction, additional defensive weapons would be sent to Ukraine.

The two decisions show both the administration’s willingness to interrupt support and its willingness to restore or expand defensive aid. The relevant unresolved issue is not whether both occurred, but what items were withheld, for how long, and with what operational consequences.

## 4.4 PURL: European financing of U.S. weapons

Trump and NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte agreed on July 14 to establish the Prioritised Ukraine Requirements List. European allies and Canada financed packages of U.S. equipment identified as urgent Ukrainian needs. NATO coordinated delivery.

By December 2025, NATO said allies and partners had pledged more than $4 billion under PURL. This structure advanced Trump’s burden-sharing objective while continuing the flow of U.S.-made air-defense and other equipment to Ukraine.

# 5. August–December 2025: pressure on Russian oil and summit diplomacy

## 5.1 Tariff pressure connected to Russian oil

On August 6, Trump imposed an additional 25 percent tariff on imports from India because India was importing Russian oil. The order described Russia’s conduct in Ukraine as an ongoing national-security and foreign-policy threat and authorized examination of other countries purchasing Russian oil.

This was an indirect sanction rather than a direct new tariff on Russia, and its broader trade consequences require separate analysis. Nonetheless, it imposed a real cost on a major purchaser with the stated purpose of reducing support for Russia’s war economy.

In February 2026, Trump removed the additional tariff after India committed to stop directly or indirectly importing Russian oil. That sequence is evidence of applied pressure, conditional relief, and an asserted behavioral result.

## 5.2 Alaska summit

Trump met Putin at Joint Base Elmendorf-Richardson in Anchorage on August 15. The summit re-legitimized direct presidential engagement and gave Putin prominent diplomatic treatment on U.S. territory.

No ceasefire emerged. Administration officials said the objective had shifted from an interim ceasefire to a comprehensive peace agreement. A summit without an agreement is not proof of failure or capitulation, but it did not fulfill the administration’s early promise of a rapid end to the war.

## 5.3 August 18 meeting with Ukraine and European leaders

Trump met Zelenskyy, NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte, and major European leaders at the White House on August 18. Official photographs show Trump calling Putin while the Ukrainian and European leaders waited.

The next day, Trump said the contemplated settlement would not include Ukrainian NATO membership, would rely heavily on European security support, and would not place U.S. troops on Ukraine’s border. Excluding NATO membership aligned with a longstanding Russian demand. European security guarantees and continued military support, however, cut against a settlement leaving Ukraine defenseless.

## 5.4 Geneva framework and December consultations

A November 23 White House readout said U.S. and Ukrainian delegations reviewed draft agreements in Geneva and that Ukrainian sovereignty, security guarantees, infrastructure protection, navigation, and economic development were addressed. Because this was an administration summary, the underlying drafts are necessary to verify the scope and enforceability of those assurances.

Trump and Zelenskyy met again at Mar-a-Lago on December 28 and held a conference call with European leaders. The continued negotiations confirm that no final settlement had been completed by year-end.

# 6. January–July 2026: continued war, investment, and a strengthened NATO framework

## 6.1 NATO support in Kyiv

On February 3, 2026, Rutte visited Kyiv and said allied support would not waver. NATO reported that PURL had supplied approximately 75 percent of Ukraine’s Patriot missiles and 90 percent of missiles used in other air-defense systems since the initiative began.

On June 3, the North Atlantic Council held the first NATO-Ukraine Council meeting in Ukraine. NATO described it as evidence of an enduring bond and visited the site of a recent Russian attack.

## 6.2 Reconstruction fund implementation

On March 26, the reconstruction fund approved its first investment, in a Ukrainian company developing communications and autonomy technology for unmanned aerial vehicles.

At its June 24 board meeting, reported June 30, the fund approved further operational measures, discussed political-risk insurance, and reported a pipeline of nearly 300 potential projects. This demonstrated that the April 2025 agreement became an operating institution rather than remaining a press announcement.

## 6.3 NATO’s Ankara Summit

On July 8, 2026, NATO leaders:

- reaffirmed Article 5;
- again identified Russia as a long-term threat;
- reported more than $139 billion in increased core defense investment by European allies and Canada during 2025;
- pledged €70 billion in military equipment, assistance, and training for Ukraine in 2026; and
- committed to sustaining at least an equivalent level in 2027.

This record is incompatible with the old Chapter 7’s speculative presentation of near-term NATO disintegration. It also shows that Trump’s burden-sharing campaign can be interpreted in two ways simultaneously: as destabilizing rhetoric that Russia could exploit, and as pressure that produced much higher allied spending and European financing of Ukraine’s defense.

# 7. Evidence supporting serious foreign-influence concern

The following findings warrant scrutiny without overstating what they prove.

## 7.1 Adoption of Moscow-preferred framing

The February 24 U.S. diplomatic shift removed explicit references to Russian aggression, sovereignty, and territorial integrity from the Security Council resolution. Moscow publicly welcomed it. This was an objective diplomatic benefit to Russia.

## 7.2 Pressure concentrated on the invaded state

The administration suspended aid and intelligence to Ukraine after a public confrontation, then restored them once Ukraine accepted the U.S. ceasefire proposal. No comparable, publicly documented U.S. cutoff was imposed on Russia because Russia rejected or delayed the same comprehensive ceasefire.

## 7.3 Economic relief offered to Russia during preliminary talks

The March 25 commitment to help restore Russian agricultural and fertilizer market access and payment channels was concrete and asymmetrical. The internal valuation of that concession and the expected Russian reciprocal acts remain undisclosed.

## 7.4 Direct, personalized diplomacy

Major policy repeatedly turned on Trump’s private calls with Putin, Witkoff’s contacts, and small-circle negotiations. Personal diplomacy can be legitimate, but limited records and bypassed institutions increase the risk of misunderstanding, manipulation, undisclosed commitments, and loss of allied confidence.

## 7.5 Acceptance of a central Russian security objective

Trump publicly ruled out NATO membership for Ukraine in the contemplated settlement. That may reflect military and political realities, but it also grants a core Russian objective before a final agreement and without a publicly disclosed substitute guarantee.

# 8. Evidence cutting against a Russian-control thesis

## 8.1 Continued provision of weapons

The administration resumed assistance after Jeddah, sent additional defensive weapons in July, and enabled sustained U.S. weapons deliveries through PURL.

## 8.2 Economic stake in a sovereign Ukraine

The reconstruction fund created a long-term U.S. commercial and institutional interest in Ukraine’s recovery and excluded suppliers of Russia’s war machine.

## 8.3 Pressure on Russia’s oil revenue

The India tariff targeted purchases supporting Russia’s economy and was removed only after India committed to cease Russian-oil imports.

## 8.4 Stronger formal NATO commitments

The Hague and Ankara declarations reaffirmed Article 5, identified Russia as a long-term threat, sharply increased defense commitments, and pledged major assistance to Ukraine.

## 8.5 No public evidence of a command relationship

No released intelligence assessment, court finding, congressional report, authenticated communication, financial record, or witness testimony from this period establishes that Russia directed Trump’s decisions. Policy alignment and susceptibility are not synonymous with control.

---

# 9. Alternative explanations

Several nonexclusive explanations fit portions of the record:

1. **Transactional burden sharing:** Trump sought to transfer financial responsibility for European defense and Ukraine to European states.
2. **Deal-first diplomacy:** The administration prioritized rapid bargaining and inducements over legal condemnation and accountability.
3. **Personalized statecraft:** Trump trusted leader-to-leader negotiation more than alliances and bureaucratic processes.
4. **Domestic political incentives:** Skepticism of foreign aid and hostility to prior Russia investigations shaped policy.
5. **Strategic coercion:** Pressure on Kyiv was intended to produce a negotiable position, while later economic pressure was intended to move Moscow.
6. **Foreign exploitation of known preferences:** Russia may have benefited from or amplified these tendencies without controlling them.

The available record does not require choosing only one.

---

# 10. Assessment

## 10.1 What the evidence supports

The evidence supports the conclusion that the opening phase of Trump’s second administration:

- gave Russia renewed diplomatic status;
- adopted language and voting positions Moscow preferred;
- exerted direct coercive pressure on Ukraine;
- contemplated concrete economic relief for Russia;
- relied on unusually personalized diplomacy; and
- was prepared to exclude Ukrainian NATO membership from a settlement.

Those facts justify serious counterintelligence, diplomatic, and congressional scrutiny.

## 10.2 What the evidence does not support

The public evidence does not establish that:

- Trump was a recruited or controlled Russian agent;
- Russia secretly ordered the relevant decisions;
- a financial quid pro quo caused a specific policy;
- the United States formally recognized Russian annexations;
- NATO was abandoned; or
- U.S. support for Ukraine ended.

## 10.3 Most defensible conclusion

The most defensible finding through July 18, 2026 is that the administration pursued a **highly personalized and transactional Russia-Ukraine policy whose early rhetoric, sequencing, and concessions often advantaged Moscow and pressured Kyiv, while later and parallel policies armed Ukraine, pressured Russian oil revenue, deepened U.S. economic involvement in Ukraine, and strengthened NATO’s formal defense posture**.

That mixed record strengthens a narrower concern—susceptibility, leverage risk, policy inconsistency, and institutional bypass—more than it supports the maximal claim of foreign command or control.

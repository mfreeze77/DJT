# Chapter 7 Primary-Source Inventory
## Trump’s Second Administration, Russia, Ukraine, and NATO (January 20, 2025–July 18, 2026)

> This inventory separates records that prove an event occurred from materials that merely describe, interpret, or advocate a position. Official statements are primary evidence of what an institution said or announced; they are not automatically neutral proof of motive, success, or private negotiating terms.

## Source tiers used here

- **Tier A:** official records, final declarations, votes, presidential actions, agency releases, audiovisual records, and jointly attributed statements.
- **Tier B:** documentary reporting that independently corroborates timing, implementation, or effects.
- **Tier C:** expert or historical interpretation used for context.
- **Tier D:** leads only; not sufficient support for a material claim.

## Records still needed before any maximal conclusion

The public record is strongest on announced policy, votes, public meetings, formal declarations, and assistance mechanisms. It remains weak on the decision process. The following materials would materially improve the chapter:

1. Signed directives and legal memoranda governing the March 2025 military-aid and intelligence-sharing pause.
2. Itemized lists of weapons, intelligence products, agencies, platforms, and dates affected by the pause.
3. State Department memoranda, cables, talking points, and meeting notes for the February Riyadh talks and March Black Sea negotiations.
4. Treasury, State, and National Security Council option papers concerning Russia sanctions relief, agricultural payments, insurance, ports, and financial channels.
5. Complete U.S., Ukrainian, Russian, and European drafts used in the Geneva and later peace negotiations.
6. Call memoranda or contemporaneous notes for Trump-Putin and Trump-Zelenskyy conversations, subject to classification and presidential-record restrictions.
7. Delivery, funding, and end-use records for PURL packages and other U.S. weapons sent to Ukraine.
8. Customs and trade data testing whether India actually ended direct and indirect Russian-oil imports after the February 2026 tariff modification.
9. Full URIF agreement, governance documents, valuation protocols, conflict rules, project diligence, and beneficial-ownership records.
10. Intelligence assessments examining Russian efforts to influence, exploit, or anticipate U.S. decision-making during this period.

## Preservation rules

- Save the complete record, not only an article excerpt or social-media screenshot.
- Record retrieval date, publication date, issuing office, title, URL, and local hash.
- Preserve audiovisual files, transcripts, captions, and metadata when available.
- Where a page may change, save an archived copy and note whether the issuer later corrected it.
- Cite the operative document before citing a press summary.
- Attribute contested descriptions to the speaker or institution that made them.

## Citation rule for Chapter 7

A claim about **what happened** should cite at least one Tier A source. A claim about **why it happened**, **what effect it had**, or **whether it benefited Russia or Ukraine** should either cite independent evidence or be explicitly labeled as analysis. No conclusion of foreign direction or control should be made without evidence connecting a Russian actor, a mechanism of leverage, and a specific U.S. decision.

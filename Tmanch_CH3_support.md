# Chapter 3 Supporting Research

## Donald Trump’s 1990s recovery: creditor control, public risk transfer, foreign capital, presales, and post-Soviet intermediaries

## Research purpose

This supporting document expands the established Chapter 3 research rather than restarting it. The central question is not whether Trump had Russian or post-Soviet financial relationships in the 1990s; the repository already documents that he did. The expansion asks a more demanding question:

> **What actually financed each stage of Trump’s recovery, and where did post-Soviet money, buyers, lenders, brokers, contractors, casino patrons, and intermediaries enter the system?**

The research separates five mechanisms that are frequently collapsed into one narrative:

1. bank and bondholder rescue;
2. foreign purchases of distressed trophy assets;
3. public-equity and public-debt recapitalization;
4. institutional partnership and construction finance; and
5. luxury-property and casino demand from post-Soviet networks.

It also formalizes the user’s “six degrees of separation” method. Connections are followed aggressively, but a chain of names remains a hypothesis until the transactions connecting those names are documented.

# 1. Expanded chronology

## 1990–1991: creditor intervention and the cash-control problem

- Trump enters 1990 with approximately $4 billion owed across more than 70 banks and hundreds of millions in personal guarantees.
- Lenders coordinate rather than pursue uncontrolled liquidation.
- Trump receives limited new credit and payment relief while banks restrict spending and force asset dispositions.
- The Taj Mahal opens in April 1990 with a highly leveraged capital structure.
- Fred Trump’s casino-chip purchase provides emergency liquidity to Trump Castle and is later treated by regulators as an undisclosed loan.
- FinCEN’s eventual enforcement record identifies failures to file casino currency-transaction reports beginning in April 1990.
- Trump Taj Mahal enters bankruptcy in 1991.
- Yacht and airline assets leave Trump’s control.

### Research implication

Trump’s immediate survival was produced through creditor coordination, family funds, asset sales, and restructuring. The casino’s opening-period BSA violations make customer-level cash records especially important because they occurred during maximum liquidity pressure.

## 1992–1993: equity concessions and refinancing

- Trump Plaza restructures through Chapter 11.
- The SEC later records principal reduction, lower interest, extended maturity, elimination of a sinking fund, and stock units issued to bondholders.
- The Plaza Hotel restructuring leaves lenders with 49% and a right to force a sale.
- Trump Castle restructures, transferring a major ownership interest to creditors.
- Trump Plaza securities are refinanced in 1993 through $330 million of mortgage notes and $60 million of PIK notes and warrants.
- Trump remains a public figure associated with assets whose creditors now hold substantial control.

### Research implication

The restructuring preserved brand value while shifting economic control. The recovery should be measured by debt relief and retained optionality, not by Trump’s public claims of uninterrupted ownership.

## 1994–1995: non-Russian foreign capital and the public-company pivot

- A Hong Kong-led consortium commits to finance Riverside South, assume or settle project liabilities, and leave Trump with a minority profit and management interest.
- The Citibank-led Plaza group seeks a sale.
- Prince Alwaleed and CDL Hotels agree to acquire control of the Plaza at a $325 million valuation.
- THCR launches a 1995 public stock offering producing $140 million gross and a simultaneous $155 million secured-note offering.
- Trump contributes specified gaming interests into the new public structure.

### Research implication

Foreign money mattered before the late-1990s Russian buyer wave, but the major rescuers in this stage were Asian and Middle Eastern investors. The public-company structure then supplied a mechanism to use outside equity and debt while preserving Trump’s brand and control.

## 1996–1997: public-market expansion and related-party transfers

- THCR issues 13.25 million shares for $386.1 million net and $1.2 billion of first-mortgage notes in the Taj acquisition.
- Trump’s Castle enters THCR through a related-party acquisition.
- Trump receives interests convertible into 5,837,700 public-company shares, valued at $168.126 million.
- The acquired Castle entity carries negative book value of $20.714 million.
- Purchase accounting records $196.109 million above the fair value of net assets acquired.
- Trump’s beneficial interest rises from approximately 25% to 36.6%.
- Shareholders allege excessive price and self-dealing; THCR denies wrongdoing.
- THCR’s stock falls sharply from its 1996 highs.
- Trump returns to Moscow with Howard Lorber and a business-access network involving Bennett LeBow, Liggett-Ducat/Vector, and David Geovanis.

### Research implication

The 1996 period joins two different leverage systems:

1. public-market capital and related-party asset transfers; and
2. renewed post-Soviet business access.

Neither proves the other caused it, but both increased Trump’s dependence on external capital and intermediary networks.

## 1996–1998: construction finance and partnership leverage

- ULLICO supplies loans for 40 Wall Street with a union-labor condition reported in recorded mortgage documents.
- The loans are later assigned to a Deutsche Bank affiliate, which supplies additional funds.
- Trump and Conseco acquire the General Motors Building with Conseco contributing most of the equity and the transaction carrying a roughly $700 million mortgage.
- Trump World Tower receives approximately $295 million in construction financing from ULLICO and a co-lender; HypoVereinsbank and later Deutsche Bank appear in the financing chain.
- Trump contributes comparatively limited capital while supplying the project concept, sponsor role, management, publicity, and brand.

### Research implication

The lender–contractor link is not merely hypothetical. ULLICO’s model openly required union construction. The unanswered question is whether required or preferred labor networks overlapped with earlier cartel-connected suppliers, and whether any lender, investor, or buyer received reciprocal business elsewhere.

## 1998–2000: presales and post-Soviet buyer finance

- Trump World Tower presales begin in October 1998.
- In April 1999, the Uzan brothers and an associate sign seven preconstruction purchase agreements.
- Four penthouses are discounted by more than $7 million to approximately $32 million total.
- Buyers receive staged-payment terms and unusual rights to advertise, assign, or resell before closing.
- The disputed penthouse deposits total approximately $8 million and are placed in escrow.
- Sam Kislin’s Trans Commodities is publicly linked in 1999 to FBI and Interpol allegations involving post-Soviet organized-crime and money-laundering networks; Kislin denies wrongdoing and is not charged.
- Property reporting later identifies Kislin as a private mortgage lender to post-Soviet Trump World Tower purchasers.
- Tamir Sapir, Kislin’s former Joy-Lud associate, uses Soviet commodity relationships to build a Manhattan real-estate fortune.
- The organized Russian sales effort at Trump World Tower expands after completion, into the early 2000s.

### Research implication

The late-1990s origin of the model can be established even though many closings occurred after 2000. The presale period matters because it created market commitments before completion and because some of the same intermediaries trace back to Trump’s early Soviet-facing business network.

# 3. Public-company capital and related-party risk transfer

## 3.1 1995 offerings

The June 1995 structure included:

- 10 million shares at $14, producing $140 million gross;
- $155 million of 15.5% senior secured notes;
- Trump contribution of ownership interests into THCR Holdings; and
- use of proceeds for casino obligations, expansion, and new gaming ventures.

Source: [SEC 1996 Form 10-K](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/943320/0000940180-97-000299.txt).

## 3.2 1996 Taj capital stack

The SEC records:

- 13.25 million new shares;
- $386.1 million net equity proceeds;
- $1.2 billion of 11.25% first-mortgage notes; and
- retirement or refinancing of existing obligations.

The capital supplied by public investors was far larger than Trump’s new cash contribution.

## 3.3 Castle acquisition accounting

| Item | SEC-recorded amount |
|---|---:|
| Trump interests convertible into THCR shares | 5,837,700 shares |
| Assigned value of Trump contribution | $168.126 million |
| Negative Castle book value | $20.714 million |
| Fees and transaction expenses | $5.5 million |
| Excess purchase price allocated to land/building | $196.109 million |
| Trump beneficial interest after transaction | 36.6% |

Shareholder complaints alleged an excessive self-dealing price and misleading proxy statements. The company denied those claims. The lawsuit’s final disposition must be retrieved before characterizing the allegations as proven.

## 3.4 Stockholder exposure

The SEC market table shows THCR common stock trading as high as $35.50 in the second quarter of 1996 and as low as $8.75 by March 25, 1997. Later filings show prices below $1 during 2001.

This supports a risk-allocation finding:

- public capital expanded the company;
- Trump preserved a major voting and economic position;
- the operating entities remained highly leveraged; and
- public shareholders absorbed a large market-value decline.

It does not alone establish securities fraud.

# 4. Foreign rescue and partnership matrix

## 4.1 Riverside South

Documented economic effect:

- outside investors finance development and address existing debt;
- Trump retains approximately 30% of future profits and management/promotional value;
- the project can proceed without Trump supplying the full capital.

Research files needed:

- original partnership and contribution agreements;
- debt-purchase and settlement documents;
- management agreement;
- profit waterfall;
- capital calls;
- lender releases;
- side letters; and
- investor beneficial ownership.

## 4.2 Plaza Hotel

Documented economic effect:

- creditor group converts from controlling workout position toward a sale;
- Saudi and Singaporean investors acquire control;
- mortgage and renovation obligations are supported;
- Trump retains only a limited interest or development role.

Research files needed:

- 1992 restructuring agreement;
- 1995 purchase and partnership agreements;
- mortgage modification;
- renovation commitment;
- management agreement;
- retained Trump interest; and
- any future-development rights.

## 4.3 GM Building

Documented economic effect:

- Conseco supplies most reported equity;
- Trump supplies a much smaller contribution;
- the acquisition relies on a roughly $700 million mortgage;
- Trump supplies brand, redevelopment, publicity, and operating influence;
- later arbitration and litigation concern control and profit allocation.

The model resembles a leveraged brand-and-management partnership rather than a conventional Trump-funded purchase.

# 5. Trump World Tower capital-stack reconstruction

## 5.1 Known actors

- **Sponsor:** 845 UN Limited Partnership
- **Managing general partner:** Donald Trump
- **Institutional construction lenders:** ULLICO and a co-lender; HypoVereinsbank appears in the chain
- **Later lender or assignee:** Deutsche Bank
- **Major preconstruction purchasers:** Cem Uzan, Hakan Uzan, and Antonio Betancourt
- **Private mortgage intermediary identified in later records:** Sam Kislin/Trans Commodities
- **Russian sales intermediaries after completion:** Sotheby’s International Realty and Kirsanova Realty, according to reporting

## 5.2 Uzan terms

| Term | Court finding |
|---|---|
| Purchase period | April 1999 |
| Number of contracts | Seven |
| Project status | Not yet constructed |
| Four-penthouse negotiated price | Approximately $32 million |
| Discount from plan price | More than $7 million |
| Deposit | 25% through staged payments |
| Deposit treatment | Approximately $8 million in escrow for disputed penthouses |
| Special rights | Advertising, conditional assignment, resale before closing, linked termination rights |
| First projected closing | April 2001 |
| Completed related closings | Three units in July 2001 |

Primary source: [*Uzan v. 845 UN Limited Partnership*](https://www.nycourts.gov/Reporter/3dseries/2004/2004_05172.htm).

## 5.3 What the presale proves

Established:

- Trump sought multi-unit foreign purchasers before construction.
- Bulk purchasers could receive major discounts and nonstandard transfer rights.
- Buyers committed substantial deposits.
- Presale contracts could be speculative assets because buyers could seek assignment or resale.

Not yet established:

- that deposits were directly spent on construction;
- that the lender required a specific number or value of presales;
- that any buyer was recruited by a lender, contractor, government, or intelligence service;
- that Trump knew of later criminal findings concerning any purchaser; or
- that bulk discounts were improper rather than negotiated commercial concessions.

## 5.4 Escrow and construction-funding issue

New York rules generally require purchaser deposits to remain in segregated escrow unless authorized release conditions or substitute protections exist. See [13 NYCRR § 18.3](https://www.law.cornell.edu/regulations/new-york/13-NYCRR-18.3).

Therefore, “prebuying financed construction” must be tested in two separate ways:

1. **Direct liquidity:** Were deposits released to the sponsor under the offering plan, a certification, letter of credit, bond, or collateral structure?
2. **Indirect financing value:** Did signed contracts count toward lender presale thresholds, appraised value, sponsor-equity requirements, or construction draws?

The second mechanism can be economically important even when the deposits remain escrowed.

## 5.5 Construction-lender conditions

Reporting based on recorded loan documents states that ULLICO’s Trump World Tower loans required union contractors and subcontractors. ULLICO’s current J for Jobs description confirms that its construction-loan program requires union construction.

Sources:

- [The Smoking Gun mortgage-record analysis](https://www.thesmokinggun.com/documents/investigation/trump-labor-loans-408195)
- [ULLICO J for Jobs](https://www.ullico.com/products/j-for-jobs-commercial-real-estate-debt/)

The contractor condition is not evidence of organized crime. It supplies a concrete roadmap:

- retrieve the rider;
- identify every contractor and subcontractor;
- identify concrete, steel, demolition, electrical, mechanical, and façade suppliers;
- compare ownership and union relationships with Chapter 2 networks;
- identify any investor, purchaser, or lender affiliates receiving project work; and
- test whether work allocation was a disclosed loan requirement or a separate reciprocal arrangement.

# 6. Kislin–Sapir continuity map

## 6.1 Early relationship

| Node | Function |
|---|---|
| Joy-Lud | Electronics supplier to an early Trump hotel |
| Sam Kislin | Co-owner; later commodities trader, political donor, and private mortgage lender |
| Tamir Sapir | Co-owner; developed Soviet diplomatic and commodity relationships; later Manhattan developer |
| Soviet diplomats and officials | Joy-Lud customer base and commercial access point |
| Trump | Hotel purchaser and later condo sponsor/development partner |

## 6.2 Kislin’s 1990s exposure

The Center for Public Integrity reported allegations from:

- a 1994 FBI intelligence report;
- a 1996 Interpol report; and
- Russian law-enforcement requests involving allegedly laundered funds.

Kislin:

- denied knowing Ivankov;
- denied laundering;
- acknowledged employing Mikhail Chernoy;
- said signatures had been forged in a visa matter;
- supplied a U.S. Attorney letter saying a civil-penalty action would not be pursued; and
- was not criminally charged in the reported matters.

This mixed record must remain intact in the research.

## 6.3 Mortgage bridge

Later property reporting identifies Kislin-financed mortgages in Trump World Tower. This should be reconstructed in ACRIS by searching:

- Sam/Semyon Kislin;
- Trans Commodities, Inc.;
- borrower names and aliases;
- 845 United Nations Plaza unit numbers;
- mortgage assignments and satisfactions;
- corresponding deeds and transfer-tax returns; and
- related UCC filings.

## 6.4 Sapir bridge

Forbes reports that Sapir’s electronics store served Soviet diplomats and officials and that he later exchanged Western electronics for Soviet fertilizer and oil rights. He invested proceeds in depressed Manhattan real estate.

This creates a continuity chain:

> Trump hotel supplier → Soviet diplomatic commerce → Russian commodities → Manhattan property capital → later Trump development partner.

Chapter 3 should treat Sapir as a bridge into Chapter 4, where his organization becomes a Trump SoHo partner.

# 8. 1996 Moscow network map

## Nodes

### Donald Trump

- financially recovering brand owner;
- seeking Moscow development;
- experienced with Soviet cultivation from 1987;
- newly able to mobilize public and institutional capital.

### Howard Lorber

- U.S. businessman with Russian commercial access;
- later political supporter;
- traveled with Trump and participated in development exploration.

### Bennett LeBow / Liggett-Ducat / Vector

- corporate network with tobacco and Russian business interests;
- helped create the commercial pathway for the visit.

### David Geovanis

- Moscow-based American business intermediary;
- helped guide or organize the development exploration;
- later became the subject of Senate interest involving unproven kompromat allegations.

### Russian political and business figures

- officials and developers able to approve, site, finance, or politically support a Moscow project.

## Research questions

- Who paid for travel and hospitality?
- What visa sponsor, host organization, or Russian ministry approved the visit?
- What development entities supplied sites or proposals?
- Did any Russian bank or state enterprise provide financing terms?
- What were Lorber, LeBow, and Geovanis promised?
- Did Trump offer licensing, equity, management, construction, or political access?
- Were photographs, recordings, surveillance, or intelligence reports created?
- Did the parties continue communications after the trip?

# 9. Strongest expanded findings

## Finding 1: the immediate bailout was bank-led

The early rescue was accomplished through domestic creditor control, family assistance, asset sales, and restructurings. This should replace any implication that Russian condo buyers directly prevented Trump’s 1990–1993 collapse.

## Finding 2: foreign capital still played a decisive recovery role

Asian investors at Riverside South and Saudi/Singaporean investors at the Plaza removed liabilities and preserved minority, management, or brand value for Trump. Foreign dependence preceded the Russian buyer wave.

## Finding 3: THCR externalized risk while preserving Trump’s position

Public shareholders and bondholders funded expansion and acquisition. Trump transferred private casino interests into the public structure, received convertible value, and increased his beneficial position. Related-party allegations require final-case research, but the transaction mechanics are established.

## Finding 4: the post-Soviet role grew through demand and intermediation

Post-Soviet capital became increasingly useful through:

- condominium purchases;
- bulk presales;
- private mortgages;
- buyer introductions;
- casino patronage;
- Russian-facing business access; and
- intermediaries who had operated between New York and the Soviet economy for years.

## Finding 5: the Kislin–Sapir chain is the strongest six-degrees lead

The same early Trump supplier network later produced:

- a private mortgage lender to Trump-property buyers;
- a trader described in FBI/Interpol allegations but never charged; and
- a Soviet-commodity businessman who later became a Trump development partner.

## Finding 6: Trump World Tower provides a testable presale-construction model

The Uzan court record proves bulk preconstruction purchasing, negotiated discounts, assignment rights, and large escrowed deposits. Loan and offering-plan records can establish whether those contracts unlocked lender advances or merely signaled demand.

## Finding 7: financing could include contractor requirements

ULLICO’s union-labor condition demonstrates that lenders could dictate the labor network. The open question is whether any required network overlapped with earlier organized-crime-controlled suppliers or reciprocal investor relationships.

## Finding 8: criminal use and Trump knowledge must remain separate propositions

Bogatin and Ivankov establish criminal presence or use of Trump-associated property networks. They do not automatically establish Trump’s knowledge, participation, or benefit beyond normal sales and casino revenue.

# 10. Evidentiary boundaries

The expanded research does **not** presently establish:

- that Russian money was the principal 1990–1993 rescue capital;
- that the Kremlin directed condo purchases;
- that all Russian or post-Soviet purchasers used criminal proceeds;
- that Trump knew Bogatin’s source of funds at closing;
- that Trump knowingly housed or assisted Ivankov;
- that Kislin committed money laundering;
- that Trump knew of the FBI or Interpol allegations involving Kislin;
- that Uzan deposits were directly spent on construction;
- that ULLICO’s union requirement was improper;
- that contractors were selected in exchange for buyer or lender capital;
- that the Castle shareholder allegations were adjudicated as true;
- that the 1996 Moscow trip produced kompromat;
- that one organization centrally coordinated the decade’s banking, real-estate, casino, and Russian networks.

The research does establish systems through which those hypotheses can be tested.

# 11. Cross-chapter implications

## Chapter 2

Chapter 2’s financial-vulnerability conclusion should point forward to creditor control rather than jump directly to Russian rescue.

Recommended bridge:

> The 1990 collapse did not immediately place Trump under Russian financial control. Domestic banks and bondholders first preserved his brand through coordinated workouts, bankruptcies, and equity concessions. That creditor-created survival window later allowed foreign investors, public markets, and post-Soviet buyer networks to become part of the recovery.

## Chapter 4

Chapter 4 should begin with three continuities:

1. Kislin private-credit and post-Soviet buyer networks;
2. Sapir’s transition from Soviet commodities to Trump development partnership; and
3. Trump’s established preference for contributing brand and minority capital while outsiders supply development finance.

Recommended bridge:

> Bayrock and Trump SoHo did not create Trump’s post-Soviet business model. They expanded a model already visible in the 1990s: outside capital, intermediary-supplied buyers, limited Trump equity, aggressive branding, related-party value extraction, and high-end real estate as the transaction platform.

## TLDR and conclusion

Replace “Russian money saved Trump after bankruptcy” with:

> Domestic creditors and non-Russian foreign investors prevented Trump’s immediate collapse. Post-Soviet capital then became an increasingly important component of the rebuilding model, providing condominium demand, private credit, business access, and a network of intermediaries who connected Trump-branded assets to wealth leaving the former Soviet system.

## Overall timeline additions

- **1992:** Plaza restructuring reduces principal and extends maturity while bondholders receive equity-linked units.
- **1994:** Hong Kong-led capital finances Riverside South and preserves a minority Trump role.
- **1995:** Saudi/Singaporean investors acquire Plaza control; THCR raises public equity and high-yield debt.
- **1996:** THCR raises $386.1 million equity and $1.2 billion mortgage debt; Castle transferred into public structure; Trump beneficial interest rises.
- **1996:** Trump returns to Moscow through Lorber/LeBow/Geovanis network.
- **1998:** Trump World Tower presales begin; institutional construction financing includes labor conditions.
- **1999:** Uzan group signs seven preconstruction purchases; Center for Public Integrity publishes FBI/Interpol allegations involving Kislin.
- **2000:** Presale, private-credit, and post-Soviet buyer model is positioned for early-2000s expansion.

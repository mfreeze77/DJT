# Cuba–Colombia–South Florida Narcotics Route, 1979–1985: Primary-Record Tranche

## Scope and evidentiary status

This tranche identifies a documented federal prosecution that can serve as the starting point for testing the repository's hypothesis about Cuban-linked narcotics trafficking, financial proceeds, and possible real-estate intersections in the late 1970s and early 1980s.

The principal public anchor is:

> **United States v. Jaime Guillot-Lara et al., No. 82-643 Cr-JE, United States District Court for the Southern District of Florida.**

A publicly available transcription of the indictment alleges that, from approximately fall 1979 through 1981, named traffickers and Cuban officials participated in arrangements using Cuba as a loading, supply, protection, or resupply point for vessels transporting controlled substances from Colombia toward South Florida.

Public indictment transcription: [United States v. Jaime Guillot-Lara et al., No. 82-643 Cr-JE](https://www.latinamericanstudies.org/drugs/indictment-82.htm)

### Critical limitation

An indictment states allegations. It is not a conviction or judicial finding. The public transcription should be checked against a certified or archival copy from the court file.

The central indictment counts located for this tranche concern **methaqualone and marijuana**, not principally cocaine. The research should not relabel the prosecution as a cocaine case without a separate primary source.

No record presently cited here establishes that proceeds from this alleged trafficking route entered a Trump property, Trump-affiliated entity, or Trump-controlled account. That potential connection remains a testable hypothesis.

## Why this case is useful

The case provides concrete identifiers needed for high-quality records requests:

- A federal case number.
- A defined time period.
- Named defendants and aliases.
- Cuban officials and government positions alleged in the indictment.
- Specific vessels.
- Specific locations in Cuba, Colombia, the Bahamas, and South Florida.
- Travel and telephone events.
- An alleged private-aircraft movement.
- A prosecuting district and named prosecutors.

These details make it possible to request existing records rather than asking agencies to perform research or answer a broad question.

## Alleged route and exchange

The indictment alleges arrangements under which Cuba was used to protect, load, or resupply vessels carrying methaqualone or marijuana from Colombia toward South Florida. Separate contemporaneous intelligence material described reporting that Guillot-Lara supplied boats or logistics for weapons deliveries to Colombia's M-19 in exchange for Cuban assistance.

A declassified 1982 National Intelligence Council memorandum, **Challenges to Colombia's New Administration**, states that public revelations linked M-19 leader Jaime Bateman and Guillot-Lara to Cuban assistance for drug running in exchange for use of boats in weapons delivery to M-19.

Official CIA Reading Room source: [Challenges to Colombia's New Administration, 29 July 1982](https://www.cia.gov/readingroom/document/cia-rdp85t00176r001600010007-2)

This intelligence reference corroborates that the subject was being discussed within the U.S. intelligence community; it does not by itself prove every allegation or identify the disposition of the criminal case.

Additional official archival leads include:

- [Cuban International Activities Inimical to U.S. Interests, 8 November 1982](https://www.cia.gov/readingroom/document/cia-rdp84b00049r001202840038-9)
- [Statement by the Deputy Director of Central Intelligence concerning intelligence-community narcotics activities, 22 April 1982](https://www.cia.gov/readingroom/document/cia-rdp84b00049r000802020008-7)
- [Proposed Meeting with Senator Hawkins Concerning Cuban Involvement in Drug Trafficking, June 1984](https://www.cia.gov/readingroom/document/cia-rdp95b00895r000200100012-0)

Some CIA Reading Room documents are press clippings or open-source material retained by the agency. Their presence in CIA files does not convert the underlying press account into an intelligence finding. Each document's content type and provenance must be recorded.

## Phase 1 — Obtain the complete federal court file

Federal courts are not subject to FOIA. Search PACER for the case, then contact the Southern District of Florida clerk or the National Archives facility holding the retired case file.

Official sources:

- [PACER — Find a Case](https://pacer.uscourts.gov/find-case)
- [National Archives — Court Records](https://www.archives.gov/research/court-records)

Request the complete file for **No. 82-643 Cr-JE**, including:

1. Docket sheet and party index.
2. Original and superseding indictments.
3. Bills of particulars.
4. Arrest warrants and returns that are publicly available.
5. Extradition pleadings and diplomatic filings.
6. Search-warrant affidavits, returns, and inventories where unsealed.
7. Government and defense exhibit lists.
8. Trial exhibits and transcripts.
9. Plea agreements, verdicts, dismissals, and judgments.
10. Sentencing memoranda and judgments.
11. Forfeiture, seizure, restraint, restitution, and property schedules.
12. Corporate, vessel, aircraft, telephone, bank, and address exhibits.
13. Related-case and co-defendant case numbers.
14. Appellate notices, briefs, and mandates.

The case disposition for each defendant must be documented separately. Do not infer that one defendant's outcome applies to every defendant.

### CIA

Request finished intelligence, disseminated reports, assessments, and interagency memoranda rather than “all operational files.” Cite known declassified document numbers and request records concerning the specific prosecution, route, and names.

A focused description:

```text
Finished intelligence, disseminated reports, intelligence assessments,
and interagency memoranda dated January 1, 1978 through December 31,
1985 concerning Jaime Guillot-Lara, the defendants in No. 82-643 Cr-JE,
the alleged use of Cuba as a transit, loading, protection, or resupply
point for controlled-substance shipments from Colombia to South Florida,
and any alleged exchange of transportation assistance for arms delivery
to M-19.
```

Once a particular document number is identified, Mandatory Declassification Review may be more effective than another broad subject request.

### Legacy U.S. Customs records

The former U.S. Customs Service's investigative and border functions are now divided among successor components. After obtaining vessel numbers, aircraft tail numbers, port dates, seizure numbers, or Customs file numbers, send tailored requests to:

- ICE / Homeland Security Investigations for legacy investigative records.
- Customs and Border Protection for port, arrival, inspection, cargo, and border records.
- Coast Guard for vessel boarding, interdiction, law-enforcement, and registration records.

### FAA

Request or locate historical aircraft registration, ownership, operator, and enforcement records after the private aircraft's tail number or owner is identified.

## Phase 5 — Test for a property or financial intersection

The Cuba-route records should first be converted into a normalized identifier list. Compare that list against:

- ACRIS deeds, mortgages, leases, liens, and condominium records.
- Trump Tower initial purchasers and unit-transfer records.
- Grand Hyatt project entities, lenders, counsel, and public-agency files.
- Atlantic City deeds, leases, options, casino applications, vendor files, and aviation records.
- S&G Associates, Daniel Sullivan, Kenneth Shapiro, Damin Aviation, and Joseph Weichselbaum records.
- FBI, DEA, EOUSA, IRS, Customs, Coast Guard, State, and CIA file identifiers.

## What would count as a meaningful connection

A defensible intersection would include:

- The same company appearing in a narcotics asset-tracing record and a property instrument.
- A defendant, nominee, attorney, return address, title company, or account appearing in both route records and a property transaction.
- The same aircraft or aviation company appearing in both investigations.
- An FBI, DEA, or Customs cross-reference explicitly linking two files.
- A forfeiture schedule identifying a real-estate purchase or beneficial owner.
- A regulator documenting unexplained payments, hidden ownership, or a law-enforcement referral.

## What would not count

- Both events occurring in the early 1980s.
- A person visiting New York, Florida, Cuba, Colombia, or the Bahamas.
- A similar name without matching birth, address, company, or other identifiers.
- A cash property purchase with no evidence of the cash source.
- A later conviction used to infer knowledge at an earlier transaction without evidence.
- An indictment treated as proof of guilt.

## Falsification criteria

The hypothesis should be narrowed or rejected when:

- Transaction records show a documented lawful source of funds inconsistent with the suspected route.
- The supposed matching people or entities have different identifiers.
- Agency records show that the relevant investigation had no property, aviation, financial, or New York/Atlantic City component.
- The timing makes the proposed transfer impossible.
- A cited secondary source misstates the underlying court or agency record.

## Status assessment

### Established

- A federal indictment bearing case number 82-643 Cr-JE alleged a Cuba-linked route for methaqualone and marijuana from Colombia toward South Florida.
- The indictment supplies names, aliases, places, vessels, dates, and an aircraft event suitable for records searches.
- Declassified U.S. intelligence material demonstrates contemporaneous government attention to Guillot-Lara, M-19, and alleged Cuban assistance.

### Not established

- The final disposition of every defendant and every count.
- The complete financial and asset network behind the alleged route.
- The aircraft identity in the August 1980 Nassau event.
- Any transfer of proceeds into a Trump property or entity.
- Any knowing participation by Donald Trump in handling proceeds from this route.

This distinction must remain explicit throughout the research.

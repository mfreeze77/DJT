# Chapter 4 Supporting Research

## Brand licensing, Bayrock capital, Trump SoHo sales mechanics, Deutsche Bank, foreign purchasers, and the Moscow access network

## Research purpose

This document expands the existing Chapter 4 findings from the merged Chapters 1–3 foundation. It does not restart the period as a blank-slate audit. It develops the financial and access mechanisms that made the Trump business model particularly compatible with post-Soviet capital between 2001 and 2014.

The chapter’s key research problem is not simply whether “Russian money” appeared near Trump. It is to reconstruct:

1. who supplied land, equity, debt, presales, and operating cash;
2. what Trump supplied and what he received;
3. how project entities separated ownership and liability;
4. which intermediaries recurred across multiple decades;
5. how buyer deposits and sales thresholds affected financing;
6. whether private lenders or brokers connected particular purchasers to Trump projects;
7. where politically exposed or criminally connected money entered the system;
8. what the Trump Organization knew and when;
9. whether benefits were reciprocal across otherwise separate transactions; and
10. how commercial access became political access by 2013–2016.

The evidence is strongest when it identifies transaction mechanics. Repeated proximity is retained as a lead but is not treated as proof of a coordinated conspiracy without corresponding records.

# 1. Expanded chronology

## 2001–2003: Bayrock enters Trump Tower

- Tevfik Arif establishes Bayrock Group in New York.
- Bayrock places its office on the 24th floor of Trump Tower.
- Felix Sater becomes a major Bayrock dealmaker.
- Sater’s 1998 racketeering plea and federal cooperation remain sealed.
- Bayrock begins proposing Trump-branded developments.
- Trump’s business role increasingly centers on licensing, sales, management, and minority participation rather than supplying most project equity.

### Research significance

The arrangement creates a permanent physical and organizational interface between the Trump Organization and a post-Soviet development network. The office location affects access, communications, document custody, and how long Sater and Bayrock remained part of Trump’s working environment.

## 2003–2005: project entities and the first Bayrock–Trump pipeline

- Bayrock proposes Fort Lauderdale, Phoenix, New York, and Moscow projects.
- Separate limited-liability companies correspond to specific developments.
- Trump contributes brand value, publicity, sales activity, management, and negotiated economics.
- Bayrock and its partners organize land, debt, capital, and development risk.
- The Trump SoHo partnership is formed through Bayrock/Sapir entities.

### Records implication

“Trump partnered with Bayrock” is legally incomplete. The relevant unit of analysis is each contract among each project entity.

## 2005–2007: Trump SoHo financing and FL Group

- The Trump SoHo project advances at 246 Spring Street.
- The Kriss litigation later alleges approximately $275 million in iStar project debt.
- Bayrock presents four projects to FL Group in January 2007 and projects $227.5 million in profits.
- **May 22, 2007:** FL Group publicly announces a $50 million investment in four Bayrock projects.
- FL Group states the investment will use equity and debt.
- Alfa Investment Consulting facilitates the transaction and advises Bayrock.
- FL Group and Bayrock announce a 50:50 worldwide real-estate-development joint venture.
- The Kriss plaintiffs later allege FL acquired rights to 62% of project profits through a transaction sometimes documented as a loan.

Sources:

- [FL Group announcement](https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2007/05/22/96900/0/en/investment-of-50m-in-us-based-real-estate-development-projects.html)
- [Kriss v. BayRock, Document 439](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/district-courts/new-york/nysdce/1%3A2010cv03959/362805/439/)

## 2007–2011: Trump SoHo sales, effectiveness threshold, and buyer litigation

- Trump family members publicly promote the project and report strong sales.
- The offering plan requires a minimum presale threshold before becoming effective.
- Purchasers later allege that public claims of 30%–60% sales conflicted with an official figure near 16%.
- The buyer complaint alleges that at least 15%, or 60 units, was required.
- Buyers contend that absent the disputed contracts the sponsor would have had to offer rescission.
- Developers offer some non-litigating purchasers partial deposit refunds.
- In 2011, ten purchasers receive 90% refunds through settlement.
- Defendants admit no wrongdoing.
- A Manhattan criminal investigation closes without charges.

Sources:

- [2010 buyer complaint reporting](https://therealdeal.com/new-york/2010/08/03/trump-soho-buyers-sue-citing-inflated-sales-figures/)
- [2011 settlement reporting](https://therealdeal.com/new-york/2011/11/03/bayrock-group-and-sapir-organization-settle-federal-lawsuit-at-trump-soho-at-246-spring-street-give-buyers-refunds/)

## 2008: financial crisis, Palm Beach liquidity, and Deutsche Bank litigation

- Trump sells Maison de l’Amitié to Dmitry Rybolovlev for $95 million after buying it for approximately $41.35 million.
- The transaction provides a major gain during the financial crisis.
- Trump’s Chicago development reaches a severe dispute with Deutsche Bank over construction debt.
- Trump invokes force majeure and sues Deutsche Bank for approximately $3 billion.
- The bank seeks repayment and enforcement of Trump’s personal guarantee.
- The parties later settle.

### Research significance

The same year contains two distinct liquidity mechanisms:

1. a very large foreign property purchase; and
2. adversarial restructuring of institutional construction debt.

No public record presently joins the two transactions.

## 2009–2012: Sater’s status becomes visible and Deutsche lending resumes

- Sater is sentenced in 2009 after long-running secret federal cooperation.
- Public disclosure of the cooperation follows an accidental unsealing in 2012.
- Deutsche Bank’s private-wealth unit provides new Trump-related credit after the earlier commercial-real-estate dispute.
- Doral closes on a $125 million Deutsche Bank loan in June 2012.
- Trump Chicago is refinanced through a $130 million Deutsche Bank loan in November 2012.
- New York courts later find that false or misleading statements of financial condition were used in these loan transactions.

Source: [People v. Trump, 2025 NY Slip Op 04756](https://www.nycourts.gov/reporter/3dseries/2025/2025_04756.htm).

## 2011–2014: global licensing expands

- Trump-branded projects continue in Toronto, Panama, Baku, Georgia, Turkey, and other jurisdictions.
- The Trump Organization’s economics often derive from licensing and management rather than land ownership or primary development equity.
- Local partners, banks, purchasers, and project entities supply most capital.
- Due-diligence risk increases because the Trump name can be licensed to politically exposed or opaque counterparties while the Trump Organization maintains that it is not the developer.

## 2013: Miss Universe Moscow

- Crocus Group and the Agalarovs fund and host Miss Universe Moscow.
- The event produces substantial Miss Universe Organization revenue while the Agalarovs reportedly sustain a large loss.
- Trump seeks a meeting with Vladimir Putin.
- Aras Agalarov participates in the effort.
- Putin does not attend but reportedly sends a letter and gift.
- Trump develops relationships with Aras and Emin Agalarov, Rob Goldstone, Irek Kaveladze, and other attendees.
- Trump Tower Moscow discussions begin after the event.

## 2013–2014: Agalarov tower terms and Old Post Office financing

- Donald Trump Jr. and Irek Kaveladze negotiate preliminary Moscow tower terms in December 2013.
- The contemplated Trump economics include a percentage of sales.
- A letter of intent is negotiated in early 2014.
- Deutsche Bank supplies a $170 million construction loan for the Old Post Office in August 2014.
- New York courts later include the loan among transactions involving false or misleading financial statements.

## 2015–2026 retrospective findings affecting Chapter 4

Later records clarify the earlier period:

- New York DFS establishes Deutsche Bank’s separate $10 billion Russian mirror-trading system.
- Reuters reconstructs at least $98.4 million in Russian purchases in seven South Florida Trump-branded towers.
- Senate investigators document the Agalarov relationship, Putin access effort, tower negotiations, and counterintelligence risks.
- New York courts establish fraud liability involving Trump’s statements of financial condition and Deutsche Bank loans.
- Almaty/BTA litigation produces a judgment against Sater and Bayrock entities in a stolen-funds case, followed by a 2026 appeal.

These later findings are evidence about the historical transactions. They should not be retroactively transformed into proof of what Trump knew at the original time without contemporaneous communications or diligence records.

# 2. Business-model matrix

| Function | Typical provider | Trump contribution | Investigative issue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Land acquisition | Local developer or project entity | Brand and project concept | True ownership and related parties |
| Development equity | Partner, investment fund, or local family | Sometimes minority equity | Source and beneficial owner of funds |
| Construction debt | Bank, fund, or specialty lender | Name, presales, guarantee, management | Loan covenants and risk transfer |
| Buyer deposits | Individual or entity purchasers | Sales platform and publicity | Escrow release and presale thresholds |
| Hotel operation | Trump management entity | Management systems and name | Fee flow and termination rights |
| Condominium sales | Project sponsor and brokers | Trump family appearances and marketing | Sales claims, commissions, referral networks |
| International access | Local partner or politically connected host | Celebrity and prospective investment | Political benefit and reciprocity |
| Downside risk | Lenders, developers, buyers, public investors | Often limited by licensing structure | Whether fees survived project failure |

## Core implication

A project can produce Trump revenue and foreign access without a direct foreign loan to Donald Trump. Therefore the search must include licensing fees, management fees, sales commissions, equity distributions, expense reimbursements, guarantees, and side agreements—not only formal debt.

# 3. Bayrock corporate and capital map

## 3.1 Principal people

### Tevfik Arif

- Founder and principal of Bayrock.
- Former Soviet commerce official from Kazakhstan.
- Supplied post-Soviet business relationships and development platform.
- Later criminal case in Turkey ended in acquittal; the allegations must not be repeated as proven conduct.

### Felix Sater

- Convicted in 1998 racketeering securities case.
- Secret federal cooperator for more than a decade.
- Bayrock dealmaker with office in Trump Tower.
- Pursued Trump-branded projects in the United States and Moscow.
- Later held liable with Bayrock entities in Almaty/BTA litigation, currently on appeal.

### Tamir Sapir

- Former Joy-Lud partner from Chapter 3.
- Built wealth through Soviet commodity relationships.
- Supplied Manhattan real-estate capital and development organization.
- Co-developed Trump SoHo.

### Jody Kriss

- Former Bayrock finance employee.
- Plaintiff in civil RICO and related litigation.
- His allegations survived in part at the pleading stage but were not fully adjudicated because the litigation settled.

### Alfa Investment Consulting

- Identified by FL Group as transaction facilitator and Bayrock adviser.
- Requires corporate, beneficial-owner, engagement, and compensation research.
- Should not be conflated without proof with Alfa Bank or unrelated “Alfa” entities.

## 3.2 Principal entities

| Entity | Reported role | Missing records |
|---|---|---|
| Bayrock Group LLC / Inc. | Upper-tier Bayrock operations | Operating agreements and ownership ledgers |
| Bayrock Spring Street | Trump SoHo interest | Capital account and project agreements |
| Bayrock Merrimac | Fort Lauderdale | Debt, land, presales, foreclosure records |
| Bayrock Camelback | Phoenix | Option and financing records |
| Bayrock Whitestone | Queens project | Land and investor records |
| Bayrock Ocean Club | Project holding entity | Ownership and transaction history |
| Bayrock/Sapir Organization LLC | Trump SoHo sponsor/developer | Offering plan and sponsor ledger |
| FL Group investing vehicles | $50m investment | Exact subsidiaries and bank accounts |
| Alfa Investment Consulting | Facilitator/adviser | Engagement and beneficial ownership |

## 3.3 Why the entity tiers matter

A high-level Bayrock profit projection does not prove that Bayrock owned the full economic interest in each lower-tier project. A loan to one entity does not prove cash reached another. A Trump consent to a project-level transaction does not establish knowledge of every upper-tier tax or ownership arrangement.

The correct reconstruction requires entity-by-entity capital accounts and tracing.

# 4. Trump SoHo capital-stack reconstruction

## 4.1 Known or reported components

| Component | Evidence | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Land/site | Sponsor/project ownership records | Obtain deed and acquisition financing |
| Development debt | Kriss litigation alleges approximately $275m iStar debt | Allegation requiring loan documents |
| Bayrock equity | Project operating agreements | Missing |
| Sapir equity | Project operating agreements | Missing |
| Trump interest | Licensing/JV/management agreements | Missing executed copies |
| FL Group | Publicly announced $50m across four projects | Established, allocation unknown |
| Buyer deposits | Offering plan and purchase contracts | Established mechanism, ledger missing |
| Sales threshold | Approximately 15% / 60 units | Strongly documented through complaint/reporting |
| Hotel revenue | Operating statements | Missing complete statements |
| Foreclosure/restructuring | Lender and court records | Requires full chronology |

## 4.2 The presale question

The buyer litigation demonstrates why sales claims mattered. A project represented as 50% sold presents a different credit and market profile from one barely above a 15% effectiveness threshold.

The key causal questions are:

1. Did the construction lender require a minimum number or dollar value of binding contracts?
2. Did contracts induced by disputed sales claims count toward that requirement?
3. Did declaring the plan effective trigger additional loan advances?
4. Were deposits held in escrow or released under certifications?
5. Did foreign purchasers receive discounts, assignment rights, buyback promises, rental guarantees, or special financing?
6. Did any bulk purchaser have another relationship with Bayrock, Sapir, Trump, lenders, contractors, or foreign governments?

## 4.3 Sales-claim classifications

- **Established:** public sales percentages were materially higher than the official effectiveness figure later reported.
- **Civil allegation:** plaintiffs asserted a concerted fraud.
- **Settlement:** major deposit refunds, no admission.
- **Criminal disposition:** investigation closed without charges.
- **Open issue:** exact speaker knowledge, contract status, cancellations, reservations versus binding contracts, and lender use of the numbers.

# 5. FL Group transaction analysis

## 5.1 What FL Group itself said

The May 22, 2007 release establishes:

- $50 million invested;
- four named projects;
- funding with own funds and loans;
- Alfa Investment Consulting as facilitator/adviser;
- a separate equal-participation global venture with Bayrock; and
- Felix “Satter” as Bayrock contact.

This is stronger than later speculation because it is the investor’s contemporaneous disclosure.

## 5.2 What the Kriss plaintiffs alleged

- Bayrock projected $227.5 million in profit.
- FL received rights allegedly equal to 62% of total profits.
- transaction documents sometimes called the $50 million a loan.
- the plaintiffs characterized the substance as a sale of profit interests.
- they alleged infringement of their own membership interests and tax misconduct.

## 5.3 What is not established

- that a court adjudicated tax fraud;
- that Trump knew the alleged tax structure;
- that FL Group’s beneficial owners were Russian state proxies;
- that Alfa Investment Consulting was controlled by Alfa Bank;
- that the full $50 million went to Trump SoHo; or
- that Trump personally received the investment principal.

## 5.4 Required funds-flow reconstruction

For each FL-related transfer:

- originating bank;
- originating account owner;
- intermediary bank;
- recipient entity;
- date and amount;
- accounting description;
- debt or equity classification;
- security or profit rights;
- tax treatment;
- onward project transfer;
- fee or distribution to Trump entities; and
- repayment, loss, write-off, or settlement.

# 7. Deutsche Bank compartment map

## 7.1 Relevant systems

| Bank system | Relevant activity |
|---|---|
| Commercial Real Estate | Original Chicago construction loan and default dispute |
| Private Wealth Management | Later Doral, Chicago refinance, and other Trump loans |
| Moscow equities desk | Russian mirror trades |
| London branch | Offset transactions in mirror scheme |
| Deutsche Bank Trust Company of the Americas | U.S. clearing and New York exposure |
| Credit-risk and syndication functions | Potential transfer or participation in Trump loans |

## 7.2 Established facts

- Trump and Deutsche Bank engaged in severe Chicago loan litigation.
- The relationship later resumed through another business unit.
- Trump’s statements of financial condition were used in major private-bank loans.
- New York courts preserved fraud liability concerning those statements.
- DFS independently established massive Russian AML failures in different bank operations.

## 7.3 Unresolved bridge

The mirror-trading and Trump-loan stories become connected only if records show overlap in:

- personnel;
- accounts;
- loan participation;
- collateral;
- guarantors;
- correspondent banks;
- source of wealth;
- beneficial ownership;
- risk-transfer counterparties; or
- internal compliance investigations.

Absent that bridge, the correct formulation is: **the same global bank had both relationships, but direct funds-flow overlap is unproven.**

# 9. Comparative licensing-risk cases

## 9.1 Baku

### Established or strongly documented

- Trump Organization licensed and participated in a project with the Mammadov family.
- The family was politically exposed through Azerbaijan’s transportation minister.
- Ivanka Trump had significant design involvement.
- Trump counsel acknowledged learning of suspected links to Iranian Revolutionary Guard-associated businesses in 2015.
- Trump entities received fees.

### Not established

- that Trump intentionally worked for the IRGC;
- that fees came from sanctioned money;
- that an FCPA violation was adjudicated; or
- that Trump controlled local construction payments.

### Records needed

- license and management agreements;
- due-diligence reports;
- accounting-access requests;
- bank wires;
- invoices;
- contractor ownership;
- source of funds;
- internal risk memoranda; and
- termination communications.

## 9.2 Panama

### Established through investigative reconstruction

- numerous unit-specific special-purpose companies;
- anonymous nominee structures;
- reported bulk purchases and flips;
- cash and bearer-share warning signs;
- Trump licensing and management economics.

### Research value

Panama supplies a comparative model for testing whether shell-company presales were treated as market demand, whether brokers were compensated without source-of-funds review, and whether project debt depended on those sales.

## 9.3 Toronto

### Established

- Trump was primarily licensor/manager rather than principal developer.
- Shnaider obtained major proceeds from a steel-company sale financed by VEB.

### Conflicting evidence

A lawyer initially linked approximately $15 million of proceeds to the Toronto project and later withdrew the statement.

### Proper classification

Unresolved indirect-capital pathway requiring bank tracing.

# 11. Strongest expanded findings

## Finding 1: the business model itself was the access mechanism

Trump did not need a direct Russian loan for foreign capital to matter. Licensing, management, sales commissions, minority interests, expense reimbursements, and high-value property sales all provided financial benefits while local partners supplied primary capital.

## Finding 2: Bayrock concentrated several risk categories inside Trump Tower

Bayrock combined post-Soviet development access, layered entities, Sater’s criminal/cooperation history, foreign investment, and global project ambitions from offices inside Trump’s headquarters.

## Finding 3: FL Group’s investment is real; the alleged tax scheme is not adjudicated

The $50 million and global joint venture are contemporaneously established. The 62% profit-interest and tax allegations require the actual contracts and were not reduced to a final merits judgment.

## Finding 4: Trump SoHo presales were not incidental marketing

They affected offering-plan effectiveness, buyer rescission, marketability, and potentially lender risk. The disputed sales claims must be compared with contract and lender data month by month.

## Finding 5: later litigation materially strengthens the Sater/Bayrock money-laundering inquiry

The Almaty/BTA case progressed to jury verdict and judgment, now on appeal. This is no longer only a journalist’s or former employee’s theory. Trump’s knowledge remains a separate, unproven element.

## Finding 6: Deutsche Bank should be analyzed as multiple internal systems

The bank’s Russian AML failures and Trump lending are both established. A transaction-level bridge between them remains missing.

## Finding 7: Russian demand was a known and monetized constituency

Reuters’ deed-level work and Trump Jr.’s remarks demonstrate that Russian/post-Soviet purchasers were important to Trump-branded property sales. Exact revenue to Trump requires project contracts.

## Finding 8: the Rybolovlev sale was a significant liquidity event but not self-proving corruption

The price and timing warrant SAR, appraisal, and communications research. Later parcel values supply meaningful counterevidence against a simplistic overpayment theory.

## Finding 9: Baku, Panama, and Toronto show why indirect pathways matter

Each demonstrates how foreign or state-linked money could support a Trump-branded project through a partner, buyer, or project entity while Trump remained formally outside the primary financing.

## Finding 10: the Agalarov relationship is the clearest commercial-to-political bridge

A pageant fee and elite hospitality produced Putin access efforts, tower negotiations, continued gifts and communications, and ultimately the network that offered political assistance in 2016.

# 12. Evidence boundaries

The expanded chapter should not be read as establishing facts that remain undocumented.

- No public record proves Bayrock was a Russian intelligence front.
- No public record proves the FBI directed Sater’s Bayrock work.
- No court adjudicated the Kriss tax allegations.
- No public record proves FL Group’s investment came from the Kremlin.
- No public record proves Trump knew stolen Kazakh funds entered a Trump-related transaction.
- No public record traces Deutsche mirror-trading proceeds into a Trump loan.
- No public record proves the Rybolovlev purchase was a payoff.
- No public record proves VEB directly financed Trump Toronto.
- No adjudication establishes that Trump intentionally received IRGC funds.
- No record establishes that every anonymous Panama or Florida buyer was criminal.
- No public finding establishes that Miss Universe Moscow was an intelligence operation.
- No public evidence proves that kompromat was obtained and used.

Those limits do not erase the documented capital and access system. They define the next records search.

# 13. Cross-chapter implications

## Chapter 3

Chapter 3’s late-1990s rebuilding model should bridge directly to Bayrock:

> The same people and mechanisms that connected Trump to post-Soviet buyers and private credit in the late 1990s—especially the Kislin–Sapir continuity—matured into development partnerships after 2001. Bayrock added layered project entities, foreign investment, and Trump licensing to that existing network.

## Chapter 5

Chapter 5 should begin with the Agalarov continuity:

> The June 2016 Trump Tower meeting did not arise from a cold approach. It emerged from a relationship funded and cultivated through the 2013 Miss Universe pageant, followed by Moscow tower negotiations, gifts, and continuing family communications.

Chapter 5 should also distinguish:

- the Agalarov 2013–2014 Moscow proposal;
- Sater/Cohen’s 2015–2016 proposal;
- Giorgi Rtskhiladze’s proposal;
- other independent tower approaches; and
- the public statements made while those opportunities remained active.

## Overall timeline additions

- **2001:** Bayrock founded in New York.
- **2002–2003:** Sater enters Bayrock; offices in Trump Tower.
- **2005:** Trump SoHo and broader Bayrock partnership advance.
- **May 2007:** FL Group announces $50m investment and global JV.
- **2008:** Rybolovlev purchases Palm Beach mansion; Chicago Deutsche dispute.
- **2010–2011:** Trump SoHo buyer litigation and 90% deposit refunds.
- **2012:** Deutsche private-wealth loans resume; Sater cooperation becomes public.
- **November 2013:** Miss Universe Moscow and Putin access effort.
- **December 2013–2014:** Agalarov Moscow tower negotiations.
- **August 2014:** Deutsche Old Post Office construction loan.
- **2024–2026 retrospective:** Almaty/BTA verdict, retrial, judgment, and appeal concerning Sater/Bayrock entities.

## TLDR and conclusion language

Recommended formulation:

> From 2001 through 2014, Trump converted his name into an international platform that could attract development debt, foreign partners, preconstruction buyers, and politically connected hosts without requiring Trump to supply most project capital. The structure created recurring exposure to opaque ownership, high-risk intermediaries, and foreign leverage. The Agalarov relationship demonstrates how a profitable commercial event could become a durable political-access channel.
